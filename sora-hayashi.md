# SORA HAYASHI — The Boy on the Other End

**Age:** 8
**School:** Yanagi Municipal Elementary, 3rd grade
**Role:** The living stakes / Mira's structural twin / truth expressed as mapping instead of speech
**Reputation:** Quiet. Daydreamer. "Off in his own world."
**Reality:** A child who built entire cities inside his head because the real one didn't have room for him.

---

## THE DESIGN PROBLEM

Sora cannot be just the boy the player rescues.

If Sora enters the story only as a rescue objective — a name on a poster, an absence the investigation fills — he becomes the trope this game's structure is too sophisticated to support: the innocent child who exists to be saved by the dead girl. That's outcome logic. The player's relationship to him would be procedural, not emotional.

The fix is not to give Sora more screen time. It's to make him *part of the investigation's cognitive map* before the player knows he matters. He should appear as a pattern artifact — fragments of his presence woven into the evidence chain, unnoticed at first, accumulating into recognition. By the time Mira makes the final call, the player should feel not "we're saving someone" but "we've been looking at him this whole time."

Sora is not Mira's reward. He is her structural twin — a child on the same perceptual spectrum, expressing truth as structure instead of language. Mira reported. Sora mapped. Both saw the world with the kind of accuracy that the system cannot tolerate. The difference is that Mira was loud enough to be targeted for what she *did.* Sora was quiet enough to be targeted for what he *might become.*

The game builds him from fragments the same way it builds every truth: through accumulated weight, assembled by the player into a portrait they didn't realize they were building.

## THE CHILD

Sora draws maps.

Not real maps — invented ones. Maps of cities that don't exist, rendered in colored pencil on graph paper he takes from the math supply cabinet at school. The cities have names. They have streets, intersections, transit lines, parks, rivers, bridges. They have neighborhoods with distinct character — the residential blocks are drawn in soft curves, the commercial districts in sharp grids, the parks in irregular green shapes that suggest he's thought carefully about where people would want to sit.

He has been doing this since he was six. He has a stack of completed maps in his bedroom — forty-three pages, each one a different city, each one internally consistent. The transit lines connect. The rivers flow downhill. The bridges span actual gaps. He doesn't draw fantasy maps with dragons and castles. He draws *infrastructure.* He draws the invisible systems that make places work — the things that adults build and children are supposed to not notice.

He notices. He is, in his own quiet way, an observer. Not with Mira's precision or Mira's urgency — Sora doesn't report what he sees. He draws it. He processes the world by redesigning it, building versions where the systems make sense, where the streets connect properly, where there are enough parks and the transit runs on time and the neighborhoods are laid out so that the residential blocks are always far enough from the noisy parts.

His maps are beautiful. His teacher, during a parent conference, described them as "very detailed, but he should be paying more attention to the actual lesson." His mother nodded and said she'd talk to him about it. She didn't. She works two jobs. Talking to Sora about his maps would require looking at them, and looking at them would require time she doesn't have, and the maps are not a problem — they're the opposite of a problem. They keep him quiet. They keep him occupied. They keep him in his own world, which is where the adults in his life prefer him to be.

**Piano:** Sora takes piano lessons. Not because he wants to — because his mother enrolled him two years ago when a colleague mentioned that musical training improved academic performance. Sora is not good at piano. He doesn't dislike it; he simply doesn't connect with it. The keys produce sounds that correspond to notation on a page, and the relationship between the two is logical but not interesting to him. He attends lessons the way he attends school: present, cooperative, largely elsewhere.

He was three weeks behind on lessons when he disappeared. Not because he was skipping — because his teacher was ill and the makeup sessions kept getting rescheduled. The schedule gap meant he was walking home from the lesson building on a different route, at a different time, on a day when nobody was expecting him to be on that specific street.

Endo knew the schedule. Endo knew the route. Endo knew the gap.

**Personality:** Sora is quiet, but not in the way Yui is quiet. Yui's silence is strategic — a survival mechanism, optimized for minimum friction. Sora's silence is *operational.* He is running something behind it. Not hiding — *assembling.*

He takes things in the way a camera takes things in: constantly, automatically, without announcing what's being recorded. He watches the way buildings connect to streets and the way streets connect to neighborhoods and the way certain adults walk certain routes at certain times and the way those routes form patterns that don't match the patterns other adults walk. He processes this information not as suspicion — he's eight, he doesn't have that framework — but as *inconsistency.* Data that doesn't fit. Lines on the map that don't connect to anything he can see.

He lives at a slight delay from the world, as if everything he encounters needs to pass through the mapping system before he can respond to it. This makes him seem slow to adults. He is not slow. He is the opposite of slow. He is processing at a speed and resolution that adults stopped being capable of years ago, because adults have learned which inputs to filter and Sora hasn't learned to filter anything yet.

There is a quality to him — not precocity, not genius in any way the school system would recognize — that is closer to what anime calls "the quiet type who sees the whole board." Near from Death Note, but without the context for what he's seeing. A child who assembles puzzles he doesn't know are puzzles, from pieces he doesn't know are pieces, and arrives at shapes that look like imagination to adults and look like truth to anyone who bothers to check.

He has a combination of traits that is uniquely childlike and uniquely dangerous: imagination (he doesn't just observe — he models, he builds, he projects), ingenuity (he finds connections between things that don't appear to be connected), clarity (he records what he sees with structural precision), curiosity (he *wants to know* — not in the way adults want to know, with agenda and caution, but in the way children want to know, because not-knowing is uncomfortable and finding out is the most natural thing in the world), and desire for truth (not moral truth — structural truth, the truth of how systems actually work versus how they appear to work).

Adults don't have this combination. They had it once. Socialization, experience, the accumulated weight of learning which questions are welcome and which questions are expensive — all of this corrodes the specific alloy of traits that makes a child like Sora dangerous. Not dangerous to people. Dangerous to *surfaces.* Dangerous to the maintained fictions that communities like Yanagi depend on.

He has friends. Not many — two, maybe three. He doesn't confide in them. He doesn't need to. His inner life is the maps, and the maps don't require an audience. He is, in a way nobody around him recognizes, the most attentive person in any room he's in. He just doesn't talk about what he's attending to.

## HOW THE PLAYER LEARNS ABOUT SORA

The player builds Sora from fragments, the same way they build every truth in the game. But critically, several of these fragments appear *before the player knows they're about Sora.* They arrive as environmental noise, as background details, as pieces of evidence whose significance only becomes clear in retrospect. By the time the player learns Sora's name matters, they've already encountered him three times without recognizing it.

**Chapter 2 (environmental — unrecognized):** During the Yanagi district exploration, Kenji passes a bench near the school gate. Scratched into the wood — the way children scratch things — is a small drawing. Not graffiti. A diagram: intersecting lines, small squares, something that looks like a transit route. It's detailed enough to be notable and minor enough to pass as decoration. The player may not examine it. If they do, there's no label, no attribution. Just evidence that a child sat here and drew the kind of thing children don't usually draw.

Separately: a missing poster on the community board. Name, age, date, photograph. A boy squinting past the camera. One of several notices. Not highlighted.

**Chapter 3 (phone detail — unrecognized):** During an early call, Doi mentions something offhand. "The boy who used to sit outside my store after school, drawing. He hasn't been around." He says this not as a report — as a gap in his daily pattern. The boy was part of the schedule. Three-thirty, bench across the street, colored pencils. Then he stopped. Doi doesn't connect this to the missing poster. Neither does the player, yet.

**Chapter 5 (Haruki — named):** Haruki mentions Sora when providing school records. He describes him as "quiet, not a problem, never flagged for anything." He mentions the maps in passing — "He drew these city things in his notebook instead of taking notes sometimes. I never marked him down for it. They were really good." This is the moment Sora becomes a person rather than a name.

And then Haruki says something else. Almost offhandedly, the way teachers mention things they dismissed:

"There was this one assignment — describe your neighborhood. Most kids drew their house. Sora drew... I don't know. It looked like an engineering plan. Streets, but also lines underneath. Like pipes, or cables. I asked him what the underground lines were. He said, 'That's where the streets don't match the map.'"

Haruki pauses. "I wrote it down as 'vivid imagination.' That's what I wrote."

The player hears this. It doesn't mean anything yet. It will mean everything when Fumiko's infrastructure map enters the investigation and the cable runs under Yanagi become the architecture of Endo's surveillance. A child drew them. He didn't know what they were. He knew they were *there* — because he looked at the neighborhood the way he looked at everything, as a system, and the system had lines in it that didn't appear on any official map.

He was mapping the switchboard's infrastructure without knowing it existed. Truth expressed as structure, by a child who had no language for what he was seeing.

**Chapter 7 (the framing):** The investigation surfaces the connection Endo built between Mira and Sora. The fabricated evidence — chat logs, planted items — creates the impression that Mira and Sora had "a difficult dynamic." The player, who knows Mira by now, recognizes this as fabrication: Mira barely mentions Sora. They were not friends. They were not enemies. They were two children in the same school who occupied different orbits.

But here, in the framing evidence, the player finds something real among the fabricated: a page from one of Sora's maps. It was in Mira's locker — planted there by Endo, along with everything else. But the map itself is genuine. It's one of Sora's city drawings. And in the lower right corner of the city, there is a river with a bend in it, and at the bend there is a small area marked with two tiny figures sitting on a bank.

The river is Mira and Yui's river. The bend is the spot where they sat. The two figures are two children Sora saw once, from a distance, doing something he recognized — sitting quietly together, not needing to talk, existing in each other's presence the way people exist in a well-designed city. He drew them into his map the way he drew everything: as part of the system. As something that belonged.

Mira and Sora never spoke. But he saw her. He put her in his city. She fit.

**Chapter 9 (the framing unravels + infrastructure echo):** As the player deconstructs Endo's planted evidence, they examine each piece. The map page is the one item that doesn't fit the framing narrative. It's not incriminating. It's not fabricated. It's a child's drawing of a city that includes, in one small corner, evidence that he noticed the same girl the entire community chose not to see.

But the player who looks closely — the player who has learned to look at everything the way Mira looked at everything — notices something else on the map page. The underground lines. The same ones Haruki mentioned. Sora drew them into his imaginary city too, the way he drew everything real into his imagined systems. But these lines, traced onto Fumiko's infrastructure map, *match.* Not perfectly — he's eight, he was working from observation, not survey data. But the geometry aligns. The cable runs. The junction points. The routes that connect the community center basement to the streets where Endo's informational access is strongest.

Sora was drawing the switchboard.

Not because he knew what it was. Because he looked at the ground the way he looked at everything — as a system with components — and the components were visible if you were the kind of person who drew infrastructure instead of houses. Manhole covers. Junction boxes. The slightly different texture of pavement where cables were laid decades ago. He recorded what he saw the way he always recorded what he saw: by drawing it into a map.

The player realizes: Sora's maps are not just evidence of his perception. They are *evidence.* Physical, cross-referenceable evidence that the infrastructure Endo uses exists where Fumiko's map says it exists. A child's drawing, produced before anyone was looking for cables, confirming a pattern that the investigation needs confirmed.

Endo planted this page in Mira's locker without examining it. He used a child's map as a prop and didn't notice that the prop contained a diagram of his own surveillance network, drawn by the child he was trying to silence.

**Chapter 10 (Mira):** If the player shows Mira the map — describes it to her, mentions the river — she is quiet for a long time.

"He saw us?"

Kenji confirms. Two figures. At the bend.

"He put us in his city."

A beat.

"He didn't even know us."

Another beat. Smaller.

"He thought we fit."

She doesn't say anything else about it. But from this point, her investment in finding Sora changes quality. It was always urgent — a missing child, a ticking clock. Now it's personal. Not because she knew him. Because he knew her, in the only way he knew how to know anyone: he drew her into the place where everything made sense.

## SORA AND ENDO

Sora was in Endo's caution bucket for over a year.

Endo maintains mental categories for the children of Yanagi — the same way he maintains the community, with total attention and careful taxonomy. Most children are furniture. They exist on the surface, they generate no dangerous signal, they are processed. A smaller number are flagged: children with behavioral irregularities, perceptual sensitivity, the first signs of the clarity that, if left to develop, might one day puncture the fiction the town depends on.

Sora was flagged. Not urgently — he wasn't vocal like Mira, wasn't reporting, wasn't generating the kind of social disruption that demands immediate response. He was just... *noticing things.* The maps. The infrastructure drawings. The way he sat outside Doi's store and drew the street with a level of accuracy that suggested he was reading the environment the way an engineer reads a blueprint, not the way a child draws a picture.

Endo listened to Sora on the exchange — fragments, occasional, the way he listened to everyone. Sora didn't generate strong signal. He was quiet. His fear was low (not in danger at home, not under social pressure, not in any of the categories that produce the clarity-signal Endo monitors for). But his *attention* was high. Consistently, unusually high. A low-frequency hum where most children produced silence.

Endo filed him under *caution.* Monitor. Wait. See if the pattern develops.

The pattern developed.

**What Sora saw:**

Sora noticed that Mr. Endo visited the community center at irregular hours.

He noticed this the way he noticed everything — as a pattern anomaly. Most adults in Yanagi had regular schedules. Doi opened his store at 6. Teachers arrived at school by 7:30. The woman at the dry cleaner's swept the sidewalk at 8:15. The community operated on rhythms, and Sora — who had been mapping those rhythms since he could hold a pencil — knew them with the precision of a child who treats the neighborhood as the largest, most interesting system he's ever encountered.

Endo's schedule was mostly regular. Council meetings. School events. The volunteer programs. Predictable, legible, part of the surface. But there was a secondary pattern — visits to the community center that didn't correspond to any scheduled event. Tuesday evenings. Occasional Saturday mornings. Always through the side entrance. Always alone.

Sora didn't think this was sinister. He thought it was *interesting.* An inconsistency in the pattern. A line on the map that didn't connect to anything visible. So he did what he always did: he investigated. Not the way Mira investigated — no reports, no adults, no insistence. He investigated the way an eight-year-old with a mapping obsession investigates: he went to the community center and he looked at the building.

He found the sealed door.

Not "found" in a dramatic sense. He noticed, during a school event held in the community center's main hall, that the floor plan of the building — posted on the wall near the fire exit, the way floor plans are posted in public buildings — didn't match the building he was standing in. There was a section of the basement, visible in the plan as a labeled room ("Telecom Exchange — Decommissioned"), that had no corresponding door in the hallway. The hallway wall was continuous where the plan showed an entrance.

Sora drew this. He drew the floor plan from the wall, then drew the hallway as it actually appeared, and noted the discrepancy in the margin: *"Door should be here but isn't."*

He showed this to no one. He put it in his stack of maps. He didn't understand what it meant. He understood that it was *wrong* — that the map and the building disagreed, and maps and buildings shouldn't disagree — and that was enough to keep it in his head as an open question, the kind he turned over in the background the way adults turn over minor worries.

**What Endo learned:**

Endo didn't discover Sora's drawing. He discovered something worse: Sora's *route.*

Through the exchange, Endo monitored the ambient signal of the neighborhood. He noticed that a child's signal — low-frequency, curiosity-coded, not fear, not distress, but the elevated attention of someone *looking carefully at something* — appeared near the community center at times that didn't correspond to school events. The signal appeared on the side of the building where the exchange room's sealed door was located.

Endo couldn't identify the child through the exchange alone. But he could identify the location and the quality of the attention. Someone was looking at his door. Not trying it. Not forcing it. *Studying* it. With the kind of patient, structural curiosity that Endo recognized because he'd had it once himself, as a boy, before it was beaten out of him.

He cross-referenced. The signal appeared on days when the after-school science club met in the community center — days when Sora had access to the building. Sora's teacher mentioned, in a casual conversation Endo maneuvered into happening, that Sora had been "very interested in the building's layout."

That was enough.

Sora moved from *caution* to *immediate.*

Not because Sora had found the exchange. He hadn't — the door was sealed, the room was hidden, the eight-year-old with a graph paper obsession was months or years away from connecting the discrepancy to anything actionable. But Endo doesn't operate on response. He operates on prevention. And a child who noticed the door — a child who drew the discrepancy — was a child who might, at any point, mention it to an adult. To a teacher. To a journalist. To a detective.

Sora was the combination Endo feared most: imagination (he didn't just see — he modeled), ingenuity (he found the discrepancy by comparing sources nobody else compared), clarity (he recorded it with precision), curiosity (he kept coming back), and the desire to resolve the inconsistency (he couldn't leave the open question alone).

Any one of these traits was manageable. Together, they were Mira in a different key — quieter, slower, structural instead of vocal, but aimed at the same target. Mira saw Endo's *behavior* and reported it. Sora saw Endo's *infrastructure* and mapped it. Different entry points. Same destination.

Endo moved on Sora three weeks before he moved on Mira. He took the boy first. Then he killed the girl who might have noticed the boy was gone and *asked why.* Two cases, designed to close together. Two children with perception, removed in sequence. One loud, one quiet, both dangerous in ways the system had no category for.

Mira was targeted for what she said. Sora was targeted for what he drew. Both were targeted for the thing adults lose: the refusal to accept that a question without an answer is a question that should be left alone.

## THE FINAL CALL — FROM SORA'S SIDE

The player experiences the final call from Mira's perspective — her voice, her choice, her sacrifice. But the game needs to establish, in the moments before the call, what Sora's experience has been.

He has been held for weeks. Not in a dramatic location — in a room. Ordinary. Clean. A room with a bed and a small table and a light that is always on. He does not know where he is. He has not been physically harmed — Endo does not harm the children he holds. He maintains them the way he maintains his garden: with care, with attention, with the practical competence of a man who is waiting for the right moment.

Sora has been drawing. Everything.

On the backs of paper plates, on napkins, on the margins of a newspaper left in the room. He has drawn the room — every wall, the position of the bed relative to the door, the light source, the dimensions estimated by counting floor tiles. He has drawn the schedule — when food arrives (7:15 AM, 12:30 PM, 6:00 PM), when the light changes (the fixture hums at a different frequency after midnight — he doesn't know why, he noted it), when the door opens and closes. He has mapped his captivity the way he maps everything: completely, structurally, with the patient attention of a child who believes that understanding a system is the first step to navigating it.

He has also drawn something Endo doesn't know about. Using the sounds that bleed through the walls — footsteps above, the distant muffled pattern of a street, a vibration that might be a large vehicle passing at regular intervals — Sora has been building a partial map of the space *outside* the room. Not accurate. Not complete. An eight-year-old's best guess at what the building around him looks like, derived from acoustic data and spatial reasoning that no adult would think to apply in this situation, because no adult still processes the world this way.

The map is wrong in several places. It is right about one thing: the room is underground. He deduced this from the absence of natural light variation and the specific quality of the sounds above him — footsteps that sound like they're on a floor, not a ceiling. The building is above. He is below.

He has also drawn faces — his mother, his friends, his teacher — because he is afraid he will forget them, and forgetting is the thing that frightens him most. Not the room. Not the waiting. The possibility that the map of his life will lose its landmarks.

He has drawn the river. Once. The bend with the two figures. He doesn't know their names. He just knows they were there, and drawing them makes the room feel less closed.

When the phone rings — a phone that wasn't in the room before, a phone that shouldn't exist, a phone that rings with the sound of old copper carrying a signal it was never designed to carry — Sora picks it up because picking things up and examining them is what he does.

A girl's voice. Not an adult. A child. Younger than him, maybe. Calm in a way that doesn't match the situation.

"Someone is coming. His name is Kenji. He's grumpy but he listens. I need you to be brave for about twenty more minutes. Can you do that?"

Sora says yes. Because someone asked him a direct question and he answered it honestly, which is what eight-year-olds do before the system teaches them not to.

"My name is Mira. Please remember it."

He says: "Okay."

The line goes dead.

Sora puts the phone down. He sits on the bed. He waits twenty minutes. He does not draw anything during those twenty minutes. He holds the name the way he holds a new landmark on a map — carefully, precisely, aware that its position determines everything that surrounds it.

Kenji arrives in nineteen minutes.

## WHAT SORA IS

He is Mira's structural twin — not her echo, not her replacement, but the other point on the same perceptual spectrum. And he is, in his own way, the bigger threat.

Mira expressed truth as *language* — reports, observations, insistence, the notebook. Dangerous clarity. She saw and she *said.* The system could categorize this. "Difficult." "Disruptive honesty." Mira's threat was legible.

Sora expresses truth as *structure* — maps, diagrams, floor plan discrepancies drawn on graph paper. Quiet clarity. He sees and he *draws.* The system cannot categorize this. There is no label for a child who notices that a building doesn't match its own floor plan. Teachers call it "imagination" and move on. There is no protocol for processing a student who draws the cable infrastructure of a neighborhood without knowing what cables are for. The system doesn't have a box for this. It barely has a vocabulary.

That's what makes Sora more dangerous than Mira. Mira could be labeled, dismissed, managed. She was loud, and loud signals can be jammed. Sora is silent, and his output looks like children's drawings, and nobody examines children's drawings for infrastructure maps of surveillance networks. He is a threat the system cannot see because the system has no sensors calibrated for this frequency.

The thematic function:

**Mira:** truth expressed as language — dangerous clarity, legible to the system
**Sora:** truth expressed as structure — quiet clarity, invisible to the system
**Endo:** truth suppressed into system control
**Kenji:** truth reconstructed through investigation

This is why Endo, who has spent eleven years operating with patience and prevention, moved on Sora with unusual speed. Mira could be framed. Mira could be managed through the community's existing dismissal infrastructure. Sora couldn't be managed because nobody knew they needed to manage him. His threat was invisible until it wasn't — and by the time Endo detected the signal near his sealed door, the boy had already drawn the discrepancy that, in the hands of anyone with context, would unravel everything.

Endo is patient. With Sora, patience ran out. That acceleration — the break in his own pattern — is itself evidence the player can use.

Mira died for what she said. Sora was taken for what he drew. Both were targeted for the thing adults lose and never get back: the refusal to stop looking at something just because nobody else is looking at it.

## WHAT SORA IS NOT

He is not a symbolic replacement for Mira. He does not "continue her work." He is not the next generation of truth-teller rising to take the fallen hero's place. Making him any of these things would imply that the story requires children to sacrifice themselves in sequence, and the game explicitly refuses that framing.

He is not a child detective. The Near comparison is about *quality of attention,* not about intent. Near assembles puzzles because assembly is how his mind works. Sora maps systems because mapping is how his mind works. Neither is trying to catch anyone. Both produce outputs that are devastating to the people whose systems they happen to describe. The danger is structural, not volitional. Sora didn't investigate Endo. He investigated a building that didn't match its floor plan, because buildings should match their floor plans, and the fact that this particular discrepancy happened to conceal a serial killer's surveillance room is something Sora had no framework to anticipate.

He is a boy who draws maps. His maps are evidence — but he didn't intend them to be. The infrastructure he drew is useful because it's accurate. The floor plan discrepancy is useful because it's precise. The behavioral pattern he noticed about Endo's visits is useful because it's consistent. All of this is produced by a mind doing what it does naturally, the way a seismograph produces data about earthquakes without understanding what earthquakes are.

He is alive because a dead girl spent her last moment reaching for him. The maps and the rescue exist independently. The game does not connect them with a lesson.

## HOW SORA FITS THE ECOSYSTEM

*With Mira:* They never spoke. They share a river. She is the girl in the corner of his map. He is the reason her last moment matters. The connection between them is not relationship — it is recognition at a distance, the kind that children have when they see another child doing something that looks like it means something, and they include it in their understanding of the world without needing to name it.

Mira's final call is not about saving a friend. It is about saving a child she doesn't know, because the alternative is letting Endo's philosophy win. She spent her life being the girl who reports, who insists, who demands that adults act. Her death didn't stop this. Her final act is the purest expression of it: here is a child in danger. I am telling you. Act.

*With Endo:* Sora is the threat that broke Endo's pattern. For eleven years, Endo operated on patience — monitoring, categorizing, waiting for the right conditions. With Sora, he accelerated. He moved from *caution* to *immediate* in weeks, not months. He took the boy before taking the girl, disrupting his own sequence, because Sora's maps were approaching the sealed door and Endo, for the first time, felt the system he'd built being examined from underneath by a child who didn't know he was examining anything.

This acceleration is itself evidence. Endo's eleven-year pattern is consistent — until Sora. The break in pattern is what Fumiko notices in her historical analysis. The break is what Kenji uses to reconstruct the timeline. The boy who mapped systems produced, through his disappearance, a system anomaly that helps catch the man who took him. Sora's perception, even in absentia, is generating data.

*With Kenji:* Kenji finds Sora. He opens the door. He sees a child on a bed holding a phone that doesn't work anymore. The child looks at him and says: "Are you Kenji?"

Kenji says yes.

"She said you'd be grumpy."

This is the only moment in the game where Kenji almost smiles. The player should feel it — not see it, feel it — in the gap between the line and whatever Kenji says next, which is something practical and deflective and exactly what Mira would have predicted.

He takes Sora home. He does not discuss the phone. He does not ask how Sora knew his name. Some questions, Kenji has learned, are not for him to ask. Some answers are between a dead girl and a boy she saved.

*With the player:* Sora is the payoff. Not for the investigation — for the act of listening. The player spent twelve chapters learning to hear what people actually say instead of what the system told them was said. Mira trained them. Kenji modeled it. Every character in the failure chain demonstrated what happens when listening stops. Sora is alive because listening didn't stop. Because one person — dead, fading, using her last signal — listened for a frightened child in the infrastructure and found him.

The player didn't save Sora. Mira did. But the player built the conditions that let Mira reach him: the calls made, the reads interpreted, the contradictions surfaced, the exchange discovered, the case assembled. The rescue is Mira's act. The investigation that made it possible is the player's.

## SORA IN THE EPILOGUE

Six months later. New school. New neighborhood. His mother works one job now — the relocation assistance from the district, combined with the attention the case brought, restructured things enough that she could drop the second shift. She still doesn't look at his maps. But she bought him new colored pencils without being asked, which is more than she's done before.

Sora still draws cities. The maps are different now — not better, not worse, but changed in a way he hasn't examined. The transit lines are still precise. The neighborhoods are still distinct. But the cities have more open spaces. More parks. More places where two people could sit by a river and not be watched.

He has drawn one new map that is not an imaginary city. It is a map of Yanagi — the real Yanagi, drawn from memory, with the streets he walked and the school he attended and the route from the piano lesson that he took on the day everything changed. The map is accurate but not complete. There are gaps — places he doesn't remember, streets he never learned, blocks he passed through without mapping.

In the center of the map, where the community center stands, he has drawn a small X. He doesn't know what's under the community center. He doesn't know about the exchange, the switchboard, the cables. He just knows that something happened there — something connected to the phone that rang in a room where phones don't ring, connected to a girl's voice that knew his name before he said it, connected to the nineteen minutes between the call and the door opening.

He hasn't told anyone about the voice. Not his mother, not his therapist, not the police who asked him questions he answered honestly and incompletely. He keeps it the way he keeps his maps — privately, precisely, aware that some things belong to the person who experienced them and not to the system that wants to process them.

**The school essay:**

A class assignment, six months after the events. "Write about someone important to you."

Sora writes about a girl named Mira who called him when he was scared and told him to be brave. He writes that she said her name and asked him to remember it. He writes that he doesn't know who she was or how she called or why the phone appeared in the room. He writes that he remembers her name.

His teacher asks who she was.

Sora says: "She was the one who called."
