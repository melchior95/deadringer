# REIKO KITAHARA — The Mother Who Chose Not to Know

**Age:** 34
**Occupation:** Night-shift nurse, Yanagi General Hospital, Ward 3 (geriatric care)
**Role:** Grieving parent / the first failed switchboard / the person who had every reason to believe Mira and chose not to
**Reputation in the community:** Hardworking. Quiet. "Does her best."
**Reality:** A woman who loved her daughter in every way that didn't require confronting what her daughter was telling her.

Before the infrastructure. Before the community. Before Endo. Mira's first call — chronologically, emotionally, structurally — was to her mother. And no one picked up. Endo listens to everything but does not act. The community hears but filters. Reiko had both proximity and authority — the closest possible position, the most natural reason to respond — and still didn't. That makes her the origin point of the failure chain, not just a participant in it.

---

## THE FIRST IMPRESSION

Reiko is the first call in the game.

The player dials the Kitahara residence expecting a grieving mother. They get something more unsettling: a prepared one. Reiko answers on the second ring — not because she was waiting, but because she's been rehearsing. She has been expecting this call for three days. She has thought about what she will say, how she will say it, which questions will come and which answers will keep the conversation contained. She prepared for this the way she prepares for a shift: methodically, professionally, with the focused calm of a woman who has learned that breaking down is something you do on your own time.

She sounds composed. Not cold — there is real grief in her voice, audible in the slight thinning at the end of sentences, the way she takes an extra breath before saying Mira's name. But the grief has been organized. Filed. Put into the part of her that can hold difficult things without examining them, the way she holds patient charts and medication schedules and the quiet knowledge that the man in bed 4 is not going to make it through the month.

She provides details efficiently. She answers Kenji's questions directly. She does not volunteer information he doesn't ask for. She is, in every observable way, a cooperative witness.

She is also, in every way that matters, still lying — not to the detective, but to herself.

## THE PERSON

Reiko Kitahara is not a negligent mother. This is the hardest thing the game must get right about her, because the story will spend twelve chapters demonstrating that she functionally was one — and the player needs to understand that both things are true.

She works nights. Has for six years, since Mira was three, since the divorce, since the financial arithmetic of single parenthood in a mid-tier Tokyo suburb made the night differential the difference between keeping the apartment and not keeping the apartment. She chose the geriatric ward because it was available and because — though she would never say this — she finds elderly patients easier than the rest. They need physical care and quiet company. They do not need her to be emotionally present in the way that a pediatric ward or an ER would require. She has a limited supply of emotional presence and she needs to ration it.

She is good at her job. Not exceptional — good. Reliable. The kind of nurse who never makes errors because she never takes shortcuts. She checks charts twice. She verifies medications against the original order, not the transcription. She has caught three dosage mistakes by other nurses in the past year and reported each one through proper channels without drama. Her supervisor respects her. Her colleagues find her slightly distant but dependable. She is, in the professional sphere, exactly the kind of person you want responsible for vulnerable people.

She is also exhausted in a way that has become structural. Not the tired of a bad week — the tired of six years of sleeping from 7 AM to 2 PM in an apartment that faces east, waking to make Mira a snack before school pickup, managing homework and dinner and bath and bedtime, and then leaving at 10 PM for another shift. The tiredness has become part of her body. She carries it the way she carries her keys — automatically, without noticing, because putting it down isn't an option.

**Hobbies and texture:**

*Cooking:* Reiko meal-preps. Every Sunday, five hours of cooking that produces the entire week's dinners in labeled containers in the refrigerator. Mira's favorites were marked with a small star on the lid — curry rice, omurice, the nikujaga that Reiko makes from her own mother's recipe, which requires a specific brand of soy sauce that is only available at the larger supermarket two train stops away. Reiko makes the trip once a month. She has continued making the trip since Mira's death. She has continued making the nikujaga. She stores it in the same containers with the same star on the lid. She does not eat it. She throws it away on Thursday when the new batch is due. She has not examined why she does this.

*Drama shows:* She watches medical dramas during her lunch break at the hospital, on her phone, with one earbud in. Not for the medicine — the medicine is wrong and she knows it. For the relationships. She finds the emotional directness of television characters soothing. People on television say what they feel. They confront each other. They cry in hallways and confess in parking lots and hold each other's hands while delivering difficult truths. She has never done any of these things. She watches people do them and feels something adjacent to relief.

*Ironing:* She irons everything. Mira's school uniforms were pressed with the same precision Reiko applies to medication verification — collar flat, pleats sharp, the small embroidered name tag on the chest aligned exactly with the second button. Mira did not care about wrinkles. Reiko ironed anyway. It was the one part of motherhood where the effort was visible, measurable, and could not be misinterpreted. A pressed uniform is proof that someone is trying. You cannot argue with a pressed uniform.

She still irons Mira's remaining uniforms. They hang in the closet in order.

## THE ARCHITECTURE OF DENIAL

Reiko's failure is not a single decision. It is a structure built over years, one brick at a time, each brick perfectly reasonable.

**Brick one: the schedule.** Night shift means Reiko is asleep during the hours when most of Mira's life happens. She drops Mira at school in the morning, picks her up in the afternoon, manages the evening routine, and leaves for work after bedtime. The hours between 8 AM and 2 PM — when Mira is at school, when the things Mira reports are happening — are hours Reiko is unconscious. She does not witness what Mira describes. She receives it secondhand, from a child, filtered through the vocabulary and social context of a nine-year-old. This is the first layer of distance.

**Brick two: the pattern.** Mira reports things. This is what Mira does. She reported the boy who was stealing lunches in second grade (true). She reported the crack in the school wall that she said looked dangerous (true — it was flagged in the next inspection). She reported that the woman at the dry cleaner shortchanged her mother (true, but Reiko didn't want to confront it). She reported that Rina took her notebook (disputed). She reported that a man was watching children near the school (true, but the man was Doi, and the context was more complicated than Mira understood).

By the time Mira's reports escalated to things that mattered — a friend being hurt at home, an adult who was "always around" — Reiko had already developed a framework for processing them: Mira sees things. Mira says things. Some of them are real. Some of them are not. The cost of investigating each one is higher than the cost of waiting to see if it becomes a pattern.

This is a completely reasonable framework. It is also the mechanism by which a mother teaches her daughter that truth has diminishing returns.

**Brick three: the feedback loop.** Reiko's doubt is not isolated. It is confirmed at every turn by the community around her. Rina's parents describe Mira as "intense." The school describes her as "a handful." Aizawa's documentation describes behavioral incidents without context. Even Endo — helpful, concerned, reliable Endo — gently reinforces the narrative: "Mira is a sensitive child. Sometimes sensitive children see threats where there are none. That's not a flaw — it's just something to be aware of."

Every piece of external validation tells Reiko the same thing: your instinct to wait is correct. Your daughter is not in danger. Your daughter *is* the disruption. The community has processed this. You can trust the community.

Reiko trusts the community because trusting the community is less expensive than trusting her daughter. Trusting Mira means the school is unsafe, the neighborhood is dangerous, the man everyone admires is suspicious, and Reiko has been sending her child into a hostile environment every morning for years. Trusting Mira means the world is broken in ways that Reiko, working nights, sleeping days, ironing uniforms in a two-bedroom apartment she can barely afford, does not have the resources to fix.

She cannot afford for Mira to be right.

Not because she's ignorant. Because she understood just enough to know that being right would destroy her life. If Mira is right about the man watching children, the neighborhood is dangerous. If Mira is right about Yui, someone needs to intervene and Reiko doesn't have the hours. If Mira is right about Mr. Endo, then the man who organized the volunteer search for Reiko's missing daughter is the reason Reiko's daughter needed to be searched for. The truth, followed to its conclusion, dismantles everything Reiko depends on.

She is not someone who missed the signal. She is someone who felt the signal and chose to downgrade it.

So she chooses the version of reality she can survive.

*"I thought if I didn't believe her, the things she said wouldn't be true."*

She will say this to Kenji. Late in the game. After reading the notebook. It will be the most honest thing she has ever said aloud, and it will sound exactly like what it is: a confession from a woman who loved her daughter and chose comfort over truth because comfort was the only thing she had the energy to maintain.

## THE NOTEBOOK SCENE

This is one of the most important scenes in the game. It must be earned. It cannot happen early. It cannot happen until the player has spent hours with Mira, has heard her dry observations, has watched her read people with terrifying accuracy, has sat with her in the silence after the crying scene. The player needs to know who Mira was before Reiko discovers it.

The notebook is Mira's observation log. Not the eraser catalogue — the other one. The one she started keeping in third grade, after the second time a report was dismissed. Each entry is dated, specific, and written in Mira's careful handwriting — the handwriting of a child who is precise about everything, who still mixes up the stroke order on two kanji, who underlines important words and draws small arrows connecting related observations across pages.

Entries include:

*March 3 — Mr. Endo talked to Yui's mom after school. He waited until the other parents left. He touched her arm when she was talking. She pulled her arm back. He didn't notice. Or he pretended not to.*

*March 8 — Sora wasn't at school again. Third day. Nobody asked. I asked Ms. Aizawa and she said "I'm sure he's fine." She wrote something down after I left.*

*April 2 — The silver car was on Maple Street again. Same time. I wrote down the plate number but I can only see four of the numbers from where I sit. 47-XX. The driver doesn't get out. He just sits there.*

*April 14 — I told mom about Mr. Endo again. She said I should be grateful people like him look out for the neighborhood. I am not grateful. I am suspicious. Those are different.*

Every entry is accurate. Every entry was something she reported to an adult. Every entry was dismissed, filed, ignored, or reframed as Mira being dramatic.

Kenji gives Reiko the notebook in Chapter 11. Not as evidence — as context. He needs her to understand what Mira actually was, because he needs her help accessing Mira's other belongings, and because the investigation requires Reiko to stop protecting the version of Mira the community sold her and start dealing with the daughter she actually had.

Reiko reads it. The game does not rush this. She reads it the way a nurse reads a chart she already suspects contains a terminal diagnosis — carefully, professionally, with the discipline of a person who knows that if she lets the emotion arrive before she finishes processing the information, she will not be able to process the information.

She finishes.

She is quiet for a long time.

"She wrote this?"

Kenji confirms.

"Every time. She wrote it down every time."

Another silence.

"I told her she was imagining things. The — the entry about the car. She told me about the car. I said she was being dramatic. She went to her room and I thought she was sulking and she was — she was writing it down. She was *documenting* it because I wouldn't —"

She stops. Not because she's breaking down. Because she's doing the arithmetic. The emotional math that she's been avoiding for three days, that she's been avoiding for years: every entry in this notebook corresponds to a conversation Reiko remembers having. A conversation where Mira said something and Reiko said *not now, honey* or *I'm sure it's fine* or *you worry too much* or simply *go to bed, I have to leave for work in an hour.*

The entries are Mira's side of those conversations. The side Reiko never heard because she was already constructing her response before Mira finished speaking.

"How long did she keep this?"

Kenji tells her. Fourteen months. From third grade until the week she disappeared.

"Fourteen months."

Reiko says nothing else for a while. When she speaks again, her voice has changed — not louder, not more emotional, but *thinner,* the way a wire gets thinner when you stretch it past its tolerance.

"She was doing your job. She was nine years old and she was doing your job because none of us would do ours."

A silence. Then, quieter:

"No. She wasn't doing your job."

A beat.

"She was doing mine."

This is the structural realization. The notebook isn't evidence of a failed police system or a failed school system. It's evidence of a failed family system. Mira wasn't replacing the detective. She was replacing the adult who should have responded first — the person who slept in the next room, who made her curry rice, who ironed her uniform every morning. The role transfer is complete: a nine-year-old documenting dangers because her mother wouldn't process the reports her daughter was filing at the kitchen table.

This is not directed at Kenji. It is directed inward. And Kenji, who has spent the game learning to listen, has the grace to let it land without responding.

After this scene, Reiko becomes an active ally — but not the way Fumiko or Haruki become allies. She does not offer theories. She does not push conclusions. She does not try to investigate. She becomes something more specific and more useful: a raw data source, finally unfiltered.

"Mira told me this. I didn't think it mattered."
"I remember this now. She said it in September."
"I should have said this earlier."

She stops managing what she shares. She stops pre-processing her daughter's observations through the lens of "imagination" and "drama." She opens the archive she's been sitting on for years — every conversation, every report, every refrigerator note — and lets Kenji have it, unedited, the way Mira always wanted to deliver it.

She becomes, in the final chapters, what she should have been from the beginning: a mother who takes her daughter's word as-is. Not interpreted. Not softened. Not survivable. True.

It is too late. She knows it is too late. She does it anyway.

## REIKO ON THE PHONE

**First call (Chapter 1):** Rehearsed. Professional. She answers every question with a version of reality that has been quality-checked for contradictions. Her grief is real but managed — audible at the edges, contained at the center. She provides the timeline of Mira's last day without breaking. She mentions Mira's "imagination" without being asked — a preemptive framing, offered so casually that it sounds like context rather than defense. "She was always seeing things. Not in a bad way — she just had a very active mind." The player should note this: Reiko is managing Mira's reputation even now, even posthumously, because the alternative is admitting that Mira's "imagination" was observation.

She does not cry. She has already cried, alone, on her kitchen floor, at 4 AM, during the hour between getting home from shift and the sun coming up. She does not do this in front of people. She has never done this in front of people.

**Second call (mid game):** The rehearsal is fraying. Kenji has been asking questions that don't fit the narrative Reiko prepared — questions about specific reports Mira made, about adults Mira mentioned, about a notebook. Reiko doesn't know about the notebook. (She knew Mira kept notebooks. She assumed they were the eraser catalogues.) She is less composed. She starts sentences and abandons them. She asks Kenji what he's "actually looking for," which is the first sign that she suspects the investigation is moving toward something she doesn't want to face.

She mentions, almost offhandedly, that Mira told her about Mr. Endo. "She said he was always around. I told her that's because he does so much for the neighborhood. He organized the volunteer search after she — after." She says this as if it resolves the question. It doesn't. The player who is paying attention hears the structure: Reiko used Endo's goodness to dismiss Mira's observation. Endo's goodness was the reason Mira's observation needed to be made.

**Third call (late game, pre-notebook):** Reiko is angry. Not at Kenji — at the investigation. At the fact that it won't *stop.* She expected the process to be brief: answer questions, cooperate, be seen cooperating, and then be allowed to grieve in the specific, private, structured way she needs to grieve. Instead, the investigation keeps expanding. Keeps asking for more. Keeps implying that the story she's been telling herself — the one where Mira was difficult and the neighborhood is safe and the terrible thing that happened was random, unpredictable, unrelated to anything Reiko could have prevented — is not the full story.

"What do you want from me? I've told you everything I know."

She hasn't. She knows she hasn't. The thing she hasn't told him is that Mira asked for help. Directly. Specifically. Not in the coded, report-style language she used at school — in the language a daughter uses with a mother. "Mom, I think something bad is happening and nobody is listening to me." And Reiko said: "Honey, you need to stop worrying about everyone else and focus on school."

She has been carrying that sentence for three days. It is heavier than anything else in her life.

And there is one more thing she hasn't told him. Something smaller. Something she has been turning over in her mind since the night Mira disappeared:

Mira called her from school once. Afternoon. Reiko was sleeping — day sleep, the four-hour window between shift and pickup. The phone woke her. Mira's voice was urgent, compressed, the way it got when she was reporting something she expected to be dismissed. She said something was wrong. The line was bad — static, interference, the kind of distortion you get on calls made from certain parts of the school building.

Reiko told her to call back later.

She didn't.

Reiko has not connected this to anything. She does not know about the exchange, the old cable runs, the infrastructure beneath the community center. She does not know that her daughter was calling from a location where the lines bleed into something older. She just knows that her daughter called, and the line was bad, and she said "call back later," and her daughter didn't.

**Fourth call (post-notebook):** Different person. Or rather — the person she's been underneath, finally audible. The rehearsal is gone. The management is gone. She is not composed. She is not falling apart either. She is *working* — the way a nurse works when the situation has gone critical and the protocol has failed and the only thing left is direct, manual, imperfect effort.

She provides information rapidly, accurately, without editorial. She opens Mira's room to Kenji's investigation. She identifies items. She contextualizes notebook entries. She is the resource she should have been from the beginning, operating with the clinical efficiency of a woman who has realized that the thing she's been protecting herself from has already happened, and the only useful thing left is to help.

She does not apologize to Kenji. Apology is not what she owes him. What she owes Mira is something she will spend the rest of her life trying to calculate.

## MIRA'S SOUL READ ON REIKO

This is the most painful read in Chapter 1. Possibly the most painful read in the game.

Mira is clinical about it. Almost too precise. The deadpan that the player has been getting used to — the sardonic, controlled voice of a child performing competence — is exactly the same here. She does not change her tone for her own mother. That is the tell.

"She's scared. Not of you — of the answer. She already knows something is wrong with her version of what happened. She's been checking it for holes the way she checks medication charts. She found two. She patched them."

Pause.

"The grief is real. It's the biggest thing in the room. But it's not... in front. She put something in front of it. Something that looks like grief but is actually — management. She's managing how sad she is. She has a schedule for it."

Longer pause.

"She loved me."

This is stated as fact. Not with warmth. Not with longing. The way you'd confirm a data point.

"She just loved the version of me that was easier to take care of."

A beat. Then, almost to herself:

"She just didn't believe me enough to be afraid."

That line separates love from protection. Reiko loved Mira. But she didn't believe Mira's reports deeply enough to feel the alarm that would have forced action. Mira didn't need more affection. She needed her mother to be *scared* — scared enough to call someone, to check the school, to confront the helpful man, to do the thing that fear makes you do when you believe your child is in danger. Reiko's love was real. Her fear was managed. And managed fear does not produce response.

She goes quiet for the rest of the session. Not dramatically — she simply stops volunteering observations. If Kenji asks her something directly, she answers. But the casual commentary, the dry asides, the unprompted assessments of evidence — these go silent. The player should notice. The player should feel the space where Mira's voice used to be filling the room and wonder what it means that a dead girl just read her own mother and went quiet.

What it means is: Mira has known this for a long time. Known it alive, known it dead. Her mother loved a simpler version of her — the version that didn't report things, didn't insist, didn't make the world harder to manage. Reiko didn't reject Mira. She rejected Mira's *clarity.* She wanted the daughter without the perception. She wanted the child without the notebook.

Mira understood this before she died. Understanding it didn't make it hurt less. It just made the hurt precise.

## THE RELATIONSHIP — MIRA AND REIKO

This is not a story about a bad mother. It is a story about a mother and a daughter who loved each other across a gap that neither could close.

Mira loved her mother with the fierce, unqualified love that children have before they learn to protect themselves. She made Reiko coffee on the mornings Reiko came home from night shift — bad coffee, too much sugar, made in the small pot that Mira could reach without a step stool. She left notes on the refrigerator about her day, because Reiko slept through most of it and Mira wanted her to have the information anyway. The notes were brief, factual, written in the same precise hand as the observation notebook:

*Tuesday. School was ok. Curry rice for dinner (heated it myself, the star container). Yui was not at school again. I finished my homework. Lopsided fell out of my pocket at the river but I found him. Goodnight.*

Reiko kept every note. They are in a drawer in the kitchen, under the takeout menus. She has not reread them since Mira died. She is not ready.

Reiko loved Mira with the protective, anxious, constantly-negotiated love of a single parent who knows she is not enough and cannot afford to acknowledge it. She worked nights so Mira could have a stable home. She ironed uniforms so Mira would never look uncared-for. She meal-prepped so Mira would never eat poorly. She did everything a mother is supposed to do except the one thing Mira needed: she did not listen.

Not because she couldn't. Because listening to Mira meant acting on what Mira said, and acting on what Mira said meant confronting a world that Reiko, running on four hours of inverted sleep and the narrow energy of a woman stretched between job and child with nothing left over, could not confront. Every report Mira made was a request: *do something.* And every time Reiko chose not to, the gap between them widened by exactly the width of one ignored truth.

Mira stopped asking. Not all at once — gradually, the way you stop knocking on a door when you've learned that nobody answers. She kept reporting to teachers, to counselors, to anyone who would listen. But she stopped reporting to her mother. By the last six months of her life, the refrigerator notes had changed:

*Wednesday. School was fine. Heated the nikujaga. Homework done. Goodnight.*

No observations. No details. No Yui, no Mr. Endo, no silver car. Just the schedule. Mira had decided, without drama, that her mother was not a useful channel for truth. She loved her anyway. The love and the resignation coexisted in the same child, the way they coexist in thousands of children who learn, too young, that their parents' love has conditions it will never name.

## WHAT REIKO IS NOT

She is not a suspect. The investigation touches her — her schedule, her access, her relationship with Mira — and the player may briefly consider whether she's involved. She isn't. Making her complicit would collapse the story's point. Her role is not to have done something. Her role is to have done nothing, and to be the person for whom doing nothing cost the most.

She is not weak. She is overstretched. There is a critical difference. Weakness implies a failure of character. Reiko's failure is a failure of *capacity* — she did not have enough hours, enough energy, enough support to be both the provider Mira needed and the listener Mira needed. She chose provider. The choice was not wrong in isolation. In the context of everything else that was failing simultaneously, it was fatal.

She is not the opposite of Endo. She is Endo at domestic scale. This is the connection the player should feel without it being stated:

Endo hears everything. Reiko hears selectively.
Endo preserves voices. Reiko filters voices.
Endo prevents outcomes. Reiko avoids escalation.
Endo chooses control over consequence. Reiko chooses stability over consequence.

Same structure. Different intensity. Endo industrialized what Reiko did domestically. His philosophy — "some truths are too expensive to process" — is something Reiko has lived for six years without ever articulating it. She is not his counterpart. She is his foundation. The social physics he weaponized began in her kitchen, in the gap between a child's report and a mother's "not now, honey."

And the terrible corollary: Endo succeeded where Reiko failed. She couldn't listen fully — he could. She couldn't act — he acted, wrongly. She couldn't hold the truth — he built a system to hold it. He is what happens when someone with her constraint refuses to have it. He is not her opposite. He is her reflection, taken to an endpoint she could never reach and never would.

## HOW REIKO FITS THE ECOSYSTEM

*With Rina:* Rina's social framing of Mira — "intense," "dramatic," "misunderstands things" — confirmed what Reiko already feared about her daughter. Every time another parent mentioned that Mira was "a lot," it validated Reiko's decision to filter. Rina gave Reiko permission to doubt her own child, and Reiko took it, because the permission was cheaper than the alternative.

*With Aizawa:* Reiko trusted the school. She trusted it the way shift workers trust institutions — because she had to. If the school was handling things, then Reiko didn't need to handle them between midnight and 7 AM. Aizawa's procedural competence — the filed reports, the documented interactions, the appearance of a system that was working — told Reiko that her daughter was being tracked. She did not ask whether "tracked" meant "helped."

*With Kaito:* They never interact directly. But they share a failure mode: both saw Mira clearly enough to know she was telling the truth, and both chose not to act because the cost of acting felt higher than the cost of silence. Kaito's silence is self-preservation. Reiko's is exhaustion. The result is identical.

*With Endo:* Endo managed Reiko the way he manages everyone — through helpfulness. He offered to look in on Mira during after-school programs. He mentioned that the neighborhood watch would keep an eye out. He was, to Reiko, the proof that the system was working, that adults other than her were watching, that her daughter was safe even if Reiko herself was too tired to verify it. He was, in fact, the reason Mira was not safe. Reiko will carry this knowledge for the rest of her life.

*With Mira:* Mira's read on Reiko is devastating because it is compassionate. She doesn't accuse her mother. She *understands* her mother — the exhaustion, the fear, the grief-management, the love-that-chose-the-wrong-shape. She understands it the way only a child who has spent years watching can understand it: completely, hopelessly, without the power to change any of it. Mira knew her mother loved her. Mira also knew her mother would not believe her. She held both truths simultaneously, the way she held everything, and she wrote the notebook anyway — not for Reiko, but because the truth still needed to be written down even if nobody was going to read it.

*With Kenji:* Reiko is Kenji's access point to Mira's physical world — the apartment, the belongings, the notebooks, the room. She is also his mirror: a person who had all the information and didn't act. The difference between them is not moral superiority. It's circumstance. Kenji has the professional framework to act on what he hears. Reiko didn't. The game should make the player feel this — that Kenji's ability to help is not because he's better, but because he's positioned differently. In Reiko's shoes, with Reiko's resources, Kenji might have made the same choices. The game never says this. The player thinks it anyway.

## REIKO IN THE EPILOGUE

Six months later.

Reiko has read the notebook. All of it. Multiple times. She has cross-referenced entries with her own memories, matching dates to the nights she came home and found Mira's refrigerator notes and went to bed without asking about the day behind them. She has built, in her mind, a complete accounting of every truth her daughter offered and every time she turned it down.

She does not share this accounting with anyone. It is not for public consumption. It is between her and a daughter who can no longer hear it.

She has kept the apartment. She has kept Mira's room exactly as it was — not as a shrine, but because changing it feels like agreeing that the room is finished, that no more observations will be added to the notebooks on the desk, that the eraser collection in the dented tin is complete. She is not ready to agree to this. She may never be.

She has started volunteering at the school. Not on the safety council — that name is ruined for her now. Through the PTA, in a capacity that involves listening to children who come to the nurse's office with things they need to say. She does not give advice. She does not file reports. She listens, and then she asks: "Do you want me to stay with you while we tell someone who can help?"

The difference matters. Her failure wasn't just escalation. It was presence. She wasn't there — not physically (night shift), not emotionally (filtering), not structurally (trusting the system to handle it). She's correcting that now. Not by processing reports. By staying in the room.

She makes the nikujaga on Sundays. She makes two portions. She eats one.

---

## REIKO'S POSITION IN THE THEMATIC TRIANGLE

The game's core triangle is Mira (truth must be spoken), Kenji (truth must be acted on), Endo (truth must be contained). Reiko occupies a fourth position that completes the map:

**Reiko: Truth must be survivable.**

That is her lane. That is why she failed. That is why she's human.

## ONE-LINE SUMMARY

She heard her daughter. She just chose the version she could survive.
