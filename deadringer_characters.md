# DEAD RINGER — Character Bible

---

## MIRA KITAHARA — The Voice on the Line

**Age at death:** 9
**School:** Yanagi Municipal Elementary, 4th grade
**Reputation:** Liar. Problem child. "Difficult."
**Reality:** Observant, precise, socially unfiltered, and nine years old — which is a fact the adults in her life kept forgetting.

### THE CHILD FIRST

Before she was a case file, before she was "the girl who says things," before she was a ghost on a phone line — Mira was a kid who collected erasers.

Not expensive ones. The novelty kind sold in capsule machines outside convenience stores — animals, food shapes, tiny furniture. She kept them in a tin pencil case with a dented lid and organized them by category, not color, because color was "obvious and boring." She had forty-seven at the time of her death. Her favorite was a small blue whale with one fin slightly shorter than the other, which she called Lopsided and kept in her jacket pocket instead of the tin.

She didn't trade them. Other kids offered. She refused — not rudely, but with the firm confusion of someone who genuinely doesn't understand why you'd give away something you chose. "I picked these. Why would I un-pick them?" This was interpreted by classmates as selfishness. It was actually consistency. Mira didn't change her mind about things she'd decided. This applied to erasers, to opinions, and to the things she reported to adults. It made her extremely difficult to be around and almost always right.

### HOBBIES AND INTERESTS

**Erasers:** The collection. Sacred. Non-negotiable. She maintained a small notebook cataloguing each one — date acquired, machine location, condition, and a one-line review. "Good shape. Doesn't actually erase well. Still important." The cataloguing habit is the earliest version of the observation notebook that becomes critical evidence later in the game. Mira was always writing things down. She started with erasers and graduated to people.

**Bug watching:** Not catching — watching. Mira could sit motionless by the river for thirty minutes observing a line of ants reroute around a pebble. She found insects logical in a way people weren't. "They don't pretend. If an ant is carrying something, it's because the thing needs to be carried. It doesn't carry it and then say it wasn't carrying anything." She knew the names of local species with the casual authority of a field researcher. Pill bugs were her favorite because they committed fully to their defense strategy — "they just become a ball. No half-measures." She respected that.

**Maps and paths:** She shared this with Kaito, though they arrived at it differently. Kaito mapped infrastructure — tunnels, drainage, built spaces. Mira mapped behavior — which routes people walked at which times, where the stray cats slept on different days of the week, which vending machines were refilled on Tuesdays. She was constructing a behavioral map of her neighborhood without knowing the term for it. Her observations about the silver car were not paranoia. They were the same pattern-tracking she applied to everything, aimed at something that happened to be dangerous.

**Radio:** Mira listened to late-night AM radio on a small transistor set her grandfather gave her before he died. Not music stations — talk radio, weather reports, the shipping forecast. She liked voices without faces. She liked that the people on the radio didn't know she was listening and therefore couldn't adjust what they said. She told Reiko once that radio was "the most honest thing" because "they don't know who's hearing it, so they just say it." Reiko thought this was a strange thing for an eight-year-old to say. It was. It was also the seed of everything Mira becomes as a ghost — a voice on a line, listening to people who can't see her, reading what they say when they think they're unobserved.

**Drawing (reluctantly):** Mira drew, but she didn't consider herself someone who draws. Her sketches were functional — diagrams of ant colonies, maps of cat territories, the layout of the park with annotations about sight lines. They were not artistic. They were precise in a way that teachers found "unusual for her age" and classmates found weird. She drew the view from the bridge over the Sumida once, with small labeled arrows pointing to where specific birds sat at specific times. It is, in retrospect, a beautiful drawing. Nobody told her that.

### PERSONALITY — THE FULL TEXTURE

Mira was blunt, but she was not cruel. This is an important distinction the adults in her life consistently failed to make.

When she told Rina "you're only nice to people who are nice back, that's not actually nice, that's just trading" — she was not attacking Rina. She was describing what she observed with the same tone she'd use to describe an ant carrying a leaf. She genuinely did not understand why observations about people were received differently than observations about insects. Both were just true. Both were just there. Why did one make people upset?

She learned, eventually, that the answer was: because people don't want to be seen the way ants are seen. But she never fully internalized it. She could mimic social filtering when she concentrated — she knew the rules, she could recite them — but under any kind of pressure or genuine interest, the filter dropped and the raw observation came through. Teachers called this "disruptive." It was actually the opposite. It was the absence of disruption — the absence of the social performance everyone else maintained automatically.

She was funny, though she didn't know it. Her humor was entirely accidental — the result of stating something precisely true in a context where precision was unexpected. "Mr. Doi's store smells like old melon and regret" was not a joke. It was a sense-memory delivered with complete sincerity. But it was also, objectively, very funny. Kenji will be the first person to laugh at her observations, and the first time he does, Mira will go quiet for a moment — not hurt, but surprised. Nobody laughed *with* her before. They laughed about her. The difference takes her a moment to process.

She was stubborn in the way that principled children are stubborn — not for the sake of opposition, but because changing her position without new evidence felt dishonest. "I'm not being difficult. I'm being the same." This drove every authority figure in her life to distraction. It also meant that when she reported something, she didn't soften it on the second telling, or the third. The man watching children was watching children the first time she said it and the fourth time and the seventh. She did not learn to doubt what she saw. She only learned that seeing it didn't matter.

She loved her mother. This is essential and the game must never let the player forget it. Mira's frustration with Reiko was not rejection — it was the specific grief of a child who understands that her parent is choosing comfort over truth and doesn't have the vocabulary to say "I need you to believe me even when it's hard." She expressed this as irritation, as escalating reports, as going quiet for hours after being dismissed. Reiko interpreted the silence as sulking. It was mourning.

She was afraid of the dark. She never told anyone because it felt inconsistent with the rest of her personality and she valued consistency. She slept with the radio on — not for the voices, though she liked them, but because the sound meant she wasn't alone in the room. The transistor radio sat on her nightstand next to Lopsided the whale eraser. These were her two most important possessions. Neither was expensive. Both were irreplaceable.

### MIRA AS A GHOST — THE VOICE

Being dead has not made Mira more mature. It has made her more focused. She is the same child — same bluntness, same observational precision, same reluctant humor — but with the added weight of knowing that everything she reported while alive was correct and none of it was acted on. This knowledge sits behind every interaction she has with Kenji like a second voice she's choosing not to use.

Her deadpan is armor. It was armor when she was alive and it's armor now. The difference is that when she was alive, the armor was protecting against dismissal. Now it's protecting against something worse: hope. If she lets herself believe that Kenji is different — that this time, telling the truth to an adult will produce a result — and he fails her too, she has nothing left. No more adults to try. No more chances. So she tests him. Constantly, in small ways.

She gives him information out of order to see if he gets frustrated. She withholds a detail and waits to see if he notices it's missing. She makes an observation that sounds implausible and watches whether he writes it down or lets it pass. Each test is a question she can't ask directly: *are you going to be like the others?*

She does not test him consciously. She is nine. She doesn't have a strategy. She has a pattern — the same pattern any child develops after years of being disbelieved. It's not manipulation. It's survival behavior that persisted past survival.

### THE SOUL READ — MECHANIC AND METAPHOR

Mira's ability to read the emotional state of whoever is on the other end of a phone call is never explained. She doesn't know how it works. She didn't have it when she was alive — or if she did, it was so entangled with her natural observational ability that she couldn't distinguish between "I saw that he was lying" and "I felt that he was lying."

She experiences it as sensory data — pressure, temperature, texture, weight. Her descriptions are intuitive and metaphorical because she's a child describing something no one taught her words for:

- "He felt like holding a warm mug that's cracked on the underside."
- "She was all corners. Like a box someone folded wrong."
- "That one felt like the moment right before you drop something and you know you can't catch it."

These impressions are real but they require interpretation. A person who "feels guilty" might be guilty of what Kenji suspects, or guilty of something else entirely, or guilty of nothing and simply anxious. Mira provides the emotional weather. Kenji provides the context. The player connects them.

**Critically: Mira cannot read Kenji.** She's tried. She tells him it's like "calling a number that's always busy." She doesn't know if this is because he's the one holding the phone, or because ghosts can't read the person they're tethered to, or because there's something about Kenji she's not supposed to see. She is annoyed by this. "You're the one person I actually want to understand and you're just... static." This becomes significant in the final act — but its immediate function is to keep the partnership balanced. She can't shortcut his emotions. She has to earn his trust the same way he earns hers: by listening.

### MIRA'S FALLIBILITY

Mira is not always right. This is essential.

She misread Rina — saw deliberate cruelty where there was social reflex. She misread Doi — included him in her reports not because she had evidence of wrongdoing but because he matched a pattern she was tracking and she was nine and didn't know how to distinguish "suspicious behavior" from "sad man watching his grandson from across a street." She misread her mother — interpreted Reiko's avoidance as indifference when it was actually a different shape of fear.

Her accuracy rate is high. Maybe eighty percent. But the twenty percent matters enormously, because it's the space where the player has to exercise judgment instead of following her lead. A ghost who is always right is a walkthrough. A ghost who is almost always right but carries the biases and limitations of a nine-year-old's worldview is a partner — one the player must learn to trust selectively, which mirrors the exact decision every adult in Mira's life faced and most of them got wrong.

The game's central tension is not "should I believe Mira?" It's "how do I believe Mira responsibly?" — which is the question no adult in her life ever got to, because they stopped at the first question and answered no.

### THE ARC — ARMOR TO TRUST

**Early game (Chapters 1–3):** Full armor. Mira is clinical, detached, sardonic. She treats the investigation like a task — something to be completed efficiently. She refers to Kenji as "Detective." She delivers information without commentary. She makes observations that are accidentally funny and does not acknowledge the humor. She is performing competence so hard that nobody — not Kenji, not the player — can see the fear underneath.

- "I know I'm dead. You don't need to use your gentle voice. I'm nine, not stupid."
- "Your filing system is terrible. I can tell from here and I don't even have eyes anymore. Probably."
- "I reported six things to adults in the last year of my life. Zero follow-ups. I kept count."
- "I don't need you to feel sorry for me. I need you to write down the number."

**Mid game (Chapters 4–7):** Cracks. The armor holds publicly but slips in private moments — a pause before a read that lasts a beat too long, a comment about Yui that drops the clinical tone, a question directed at Kenji that isn't about the case. "Do you have kids?" she asks once, and then immediately: "Never mind. Not relevant." It is relevant. She's trying to understand why he listens when others didn't, and she's looking for the variable.

She starts calling him "Kenji" instead of "Detective" — once, by accident, then more frequently until it becomes default. She doesn't acknowledge the shift. He doesn't mention it. The player notices.

The mid-game break — the only time in the game the armor comes fully off — does not arrive as a single dramatic trigger. It arrives as weight.

### THE SCENE WHERE MIRA CRIES

It happens after the Yui intervention succeeds.

Kenji has just done the thing. He acted on Mira's word — no corroborating evidence, no procedural cover. Just Mira saying "she's in danger, you need to move now" and Kenji moving. He called the right people. He pushed through the resistance. Yui is out of that house.

For the first time in the game, the system responded to something Mira said. And for the first time in her life — and her death — an adult heard her, believed her, and acted. Not eventually. Not after checking with another adult. Not after weighing her credibility against her reputation. Immediately. Because she said so.

The confirmation calls come through. Kenji is writing notes. The case is still open, the night is still going. But the line goes quiet.

Then Mira says, very evenly:

"That's what it was supposed to look like."

Kenji doesn't respond. He's learned that her pauses mean something is arriving.

"When I told Mr. Ise about Yui. That's what was supposed to happen. Someone was supposed to... do that."

Her voice is steady. Still controlled. Still performing. But the player has spent hours with this voice. They know its registers. Something is different.

"I told him the same thing I told you. Almost the same words. 'She's in danger. You need to do something.' And he called her mom. And her mom said I was making things up. And he believed her mom because her mom was an adult and I was..."

She stops. Not for effect. Because the next word is too heavy.

"I was right there. I was standing right there in his classroom and I had the right answer and it didn't..."

This is where every instance of not being believed — every dismissed report, every parent-teacher conference about her "behavior," every time she said something true and watched an adult's face close like a door — arrives at once. Not as memory. As weight. The accumulated tonnage of a short life spent proving and proving and proving that she mattered, and never once having the proof be enough.

The break doesn't sound dramatic. It starts as a sound the player might mistake for static — because Mira's connection has been degrading and interference sounds like this. But it isn't static. It's a nine-year-old girl crying for the first time since she died.

"It's not fair."

Her voice is raw. Stripped. Nothing like the careful, sardonic instrument the player has spent hours with. The armor isn't cracking — it's being set down, deliberately, because she's too exhausted to hold it and this is the first person she's trusted enough to let see what's underneath.

"You believed me. You just — I said it and you did it. That's all it took. That's all it ever took. Someone just had to *do* it."

This is not anger at Kenji. This is the grief of realizing that the wall she spent her entire life pushing against was never made of stone. It was made of people choosing, one by one, not to move. And all it took was one person choosing differently. One. The simplicity of that — the sheer ordinary smallness of what it cost Kenji to believe her — is what breaks her. Because it means every adult who didn't believe her wasn't facing an impossible choice. They were facing an easy one. And they chose wrong.

"I told four adults. I told them the right thing in the right order with the right details and nothing happened. Nothing happened and I thought it was because I was wrong or because I was bad at explaining or because I was —"

She chokes on it.

"I thought there was something wrong with *me*."

A long silence. The line hums. The player sits in it.

This is the sentence that has been underneath every piece of armor, every sardonic observation, every clinical report she's delivered since the game began. She didn't develop the deadpan because she was naturally detached. She developed it because at some point, between the third ignored report and the fourth, a nine-year-old girl did the math and concluded that the common variable in every failed attempt to be heard was herself. The adults weren't wrong. The system wasn't broken. *She* was the problem. She was too strange, too blunt, too intense, too much — and if she could just be less of whatever she was, someone would listen.

She couldn't be less. So she became controlled instead. Precise. Clinical. Funny in ways that kept people at the distance where they couldn't dismiss her for being emotional. She built the armor out of the raw materials of her own personality and wore it so completely that by the time she died, no one in her life had seen her cry in over a year. They thought she didn't. She did. Just not where anyone could use it against her.

And now Kenji is here. And he listened. And it worked. And instead of relief, what arrives first is the thing relief makes room for: the full, unfiltered grief of understanding that it didn't have to be this way. Not any of it.

"And then I died. And then you showed up. And you just... listened. And it worked."

Her voice is very small now. Not deadpan. Not clinical. Nine.

"Why did I have to be dead for it to work?"

Kenji doesn't answer. The game doesn't answer. Some questions are not puzzles. They're weights the player carries for the rest of the story.

Then Mira, quieter:

"I wanted my mom to be the one."

This is the center of it. Not the system. Not the teachers. Not the procedure. Her mom.

"I kept thinking... if I just explained it better. If I said it differently. If I was more... normal about it. She'd hear me."

A shaky breath — or whatever ghosts do instead of breathing.

"She was right there. She was my *mom*. And she chose not to."

The player has talked to Reiko. The player knows Reiko loved Mira. The player knows Reiko wasn't cruel — she was overwhelmed, and tired, and afraid of what believing Mira would mean. And the player knows that none of that mattered to a nine-year-old standing in her mother's kitchen trying to explain, one more time, that something was wrong. The player holds both truths — Reiko's and Mira's — and neither cancels the other. That's the cruelest part.

Mira is crying. She's trying to stop. She's angry at herself — the way children get angry when their body betrays the composure they've worked so hard to build.

"I'm sorry. This isn't — I don't do this."

Kenji, finally: "You don't have to apologize."

A beat.

"And you don't have to stop."

Mira goes quiet. Not performatively. Not to compose the next line. She lets it be. For what feels like a very long time, there's nothing but the hum of the line and the sound of a child allowing herself, for the first time, to grieve in front of someone who isn't going to use it as evidence that she's unstable.

This is not just sorrow. It's release. The exhaustion of years of carrying the weight of being right and being dismissed — of constructing an entire personality around the assumption that sincerity would be punished — and then discovering, too late, in the wrong order, from the wrong side of being alive, that it wasn't her. It was never her. The wall moves. It moves easily. It just needed someone to push.

She is mourning her life. Not the loss of it — the shape of it. The version of childhood she should have had, where a kid tells an adult something important and the adult says "thank you, I'll take care of it" and means it. The ordinary miracle that every other child takes for granted and that Mira never once received.

And she is relieved. For the first time since the game began — maybe for the first time since she was old enough to understand what was happening to her — she doesn't have to perform. She doesn't have to prove she's credible before someone will act. She doesn't have to present evidence, control her tone, manage her affect, be less intense, be more normal, be anything other than a nine-year-old girl who saw something and said so. Kenji believed her. It was enough. She was enough.

That is what she's crying about. Not death. Not the investigation. The relief of mattering.

When she comes back, her voice is rough but steady. She doesn't acknowledge what happened directly. She doesn't thank him. She says:

"...okay. What's the next number."

And they continue.

But something has shifted. The player can hear it in every line after this scene. She's lighter — not happier, but less defended. The armor hasn't come off. But she's stopped tightening it. She volunteers things she wouldn't have before — not case information, but personal things. "Lopsided is in my jacket pocket. Left side. In case anyone finds it." She offers observations without the sardonic padding. She is, carefully, tentatively, allowing herself to exist in front of someone without justifying the space she takes up.

**Design notes:** This scene happens once. Exactly once. No player interaction during the core — no dialogue options, no choices. Kenji listens because listening is what the story is about. If the game has voice acting, this scene justifies the entire budget. If text-only, pacing does the work: shorter sentences, longer silences.

**Late game (Chapters 8–12):** Trust established, under pressure. Mira's connection is degrading. Her reads take longer. Her voice cuts out. The radio static she always associated with comfort becomes the sound of her disappearing. She is openly invested — says "we" instead of "you," volunteers information, argues about strategy.

- "I think I was hard to love. Not impossible. Just hard. And nobody tried hard enough." Pause. "That's not fair. My mom tried. She tried wrong, but she tried."
- "Kenji. If you solve this. Will you make sure they know I wasn't lying? Not about any of it."
- "I'm getting quieter. I can feel it — like the volume turning down on something that used to be loud."
- "I'm glad I called you. I'm glad you picked up."

### THE FADING

Mira's connection is not stable and it is getting worse. The game tracks this mechanically and through small details:

**Early game:** She comments freely between calls, offers observations about evidence, makes unsolicited remarks about Kenji's coffee consumption. Constantly present.

**Mid game:** She goes quiet during transitions. Asks Kenji to repeat things. Loses words — the meaning arriving slightly after the sound.

**Late game:** Entire sessions where she speaks only during reads. When she talks freely, it's shorter, stripped of decorative sarcasm. She's conserving.

- "I'm not scared. I'm just noting it as relevant information."
- "I don't think I need sleep. But I think I need something that's like sleep. Which is annoying, because I was hoping death would fix that."
- "If I go quiet for a while, don't assume I'm gone. I might just be... buffering."

### THE FINAL CALL

Mira's last act is her choice. She can feel Sora — not through a phone, but directly. A small presence. Frightened. Alive. She can reach him. It will consume whatever is left of her connection.

She tells Kenji. She does not ask permission. She tells him what she's going to do and waits — not for approval, but to see if he'll try to stop her. This is her final test, and it's the only one she hopes he fails. She wants him to say "don't go." She needs him to let her.

He lets her.

She calls Sora. The phone rings in a place it shouldn't. A child picks up.

"Someone is coming. His name is Kenji. He's grumpy but he listens. I need you to be brave for about twenty more minutes. Can you do that?"

Sora says yes.

"My name is Mira. Please remember it."

The line goes dead. Kenji's phone screen dims. The radio static fades. She is gone.

### WHAT SHE LEAVES

The eraser collection in the dented tin. Forty-seven pieces, organized by category. Lopsided the whale, found in the pocket of her jacket. The observation notebook — every entry dated, every entry accurate, every entry ignored. The transistor radio, still tuned to the AM station she fell asleep to.

And Sora, who remembers her name.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

She was nine years old, she was almost always right, and the only thing she wanted was for that to matter.

## KENJI ODA — The Detective Who Follows Through

**Age:** 38
**Division:** Cold Case / Special Investigations, Metropolitan Police
**Rank:** Detective. His department is a filing cabinet, a desk near a window that doesn't open, and him.

### THE SURFACE

Kenji is the detective who gets the cases nobody wants solved. Not because they're impossible — because solving them would be inconvenient. A landlord's son who assaulted a tenant twelve years ago. A construction company whose safety violations killed a worker in 2016. Cases with obvious answers and powerful reasons to leave them closed.

He takes them anyway. Doesn't complain about it. Doesn't make a point of it. He opens the file, reads it, picks up the phone, and starts dialing. Some of these numbers haven't been called in years. Some of the people on the other end have moved, remarried, forgotten, or decided they don't want to remember. He calls anyway. He's been doing this long enough that the sound of a phone ringing into silence doesn't bother him anymore. It's just part of the job. Most of the job, actually.

### THE PERSON

Kenji doesn't have a persona. He doesn't have a style or a method he'd name if someone asked. He's a man who shows up, does the work, and goes home. He's been doing this for fifteen years and the rhythm has worn grooves into him the way water wears grooves into stone — not dramatically, just steadily, until the shape is permanent.

He is dry in conversation — not witty, not sardonic, just economical. He uses the fewest words that will communicate the point and then stops talking. This is sometimes mistaken for coldness. It isn't. It's the habit of a man who has spent a decade on the phone with people who are grieving, or lying, or both, and learned that extra words don't help either situation. Say what you mean. Ask what you need. Listen to the answer. Move on.

He doesn't raise his voice. Not as a principle — he just doesn't see the point. Silence works better. Patience works better. The person on the other end of the line will fill the space if you let them. Kenji is very good at letting them.

He smokes. He has quit four times. He is currently on his fifth attempt, which he marks by keeping an unlit cigarette behind his ear at all times. He does not chew it, fidget with it, or draw attention to it. It's just there. When someone asks, he says "I'm quitting," in a tone that does not invite follow-up questions. He has been "quitting" for two years. He considers this progress.

He drinks canned coffee the way other people breathe — Boss brand, black, vending machine on the second floor. Four cans a day. On bad days, six. He doesn't drink alcohol. He stopped not because of an incident but because he noticed one evening that he was drinking to fill the apartment, and that felt like a problem he should solve before it became a case on someone else's desk.

He is divorced. Sayuri. Four years, ended without a fight. They didn't betray each other. They discovered, gradually, that his quiet was the wrong shape for her quiet. She wanted someone quiet *with* her. He was quiet *near* her. He didn't know there was a difference until she explained it, and by then the explanation was the goodbye. She still calls on his birthday. He still forgets hers, remembers three days later, sends "sorry, late — hope it was good." She responds with one emoji. It changes every year. This is the healthiest relationship in his life.

### HOBBIES AND TEXTURE

**Cooking (methodical, improving):** Kenji cooks from a single battered cookbook his mother gave him when he moved out. He works through it in order, one recipe per week. Page 187 of 340. His tamagoyaki is solid. His curry is genuinely good — the one dish he's made enough times that the motions have become instinct. Everything else is functional. He measures precisely. He does not improvise. He finds the process grounding: a procedure, followed correctly, produces a result. Unlike casework, the result is usually edible.

He eats alone. Always has. He doesn't mind. He minds that he doesn't mind.

**Walking:** Same route every evening. Apartment to convenience store, convenience store to the park bench facing the train tracks, bench to apartment. He sits for exactly three trains and one coffee. He doesn't count the trains consciously. His body knows when three have passed and it stands up. He has walked this route for six years. He knows the neighborhood the way Mira knew hers — not as geography but as behavior. Which lights come on first. Which dog gets walked at 7:15. Which vending machine is refilled Thursdays. He has never written any of this down. He doesn't know he's doing it.

Mira would recognize this about him immediately. She would never tell him.

**Old phones:** He keeps every phone he's ever owned. Five handsets in a desk drawer, spanning fifteen years, all dead. He doesn't know why. If asked, he'd say he just hasn't gotten around to throwing them out. The real reason is underneath — each phone held calls from cases, from witnesses, from people who told him things that mattered, and discarding the object feels like agreeing that the conversations are over. He can't articulate this. He'd be uncomfortable if someone did.

**Baseball (lapsed):** Played catcher in high school. Adequate — good patience, good reflexes, no arm. Stopped in college, never went back. Still follows the Swallows with passive attention. Checks scores in the morning. Has never attended a game alone. The idea makes him feel a specific kind of lonely he'd rather not examine. He used his old catcher's mitt as an oven mitt once. It worked. He's done it several times since. He does not find this funny. It is funny.

### THE ROOKIE LESSON

Fifteen years ago, three months into the job, Kenji caught a case. Domestic situation. Neighbors called in noise complaints. A senior detective took the lead. They talked to the wife, talked to the husband. Wife said everything was fine. Husband was cooperative, polite, offered them coffee. The senior detective said "follow up if anything changes" and they left.

Kenji had the wife's sister's phone number. It was in the file from a previous report — six months earlier, different neighbors, same noise. The sister had called in then. Nothing came of it. The number was just sitting there in the paperwork.

Kenji thought about calling. It was a cold lead. Six months old. The sister might not even live at the same number. And the senior detective had already made the call — situation assessed, no further action. Kenji was three months in. He didn't want to be the new guy who second-guesses the veteran. So he moved on.

Four weeks later, the wife was in the hospital. The sister was the one who found her.

Kenji sat with that for a long time. Not dramatically. He turned it over until it became a principle he could carry.

The principle was: make the call.

Not "be a hero." Not "fight the system." Just: if there's a number, call it. If there's a lead, follow it. If someone said something, six months ago or six years ago, treat it as though it might still matter. Because the cost of calling is nothing. A few minutes. A dial tone. Maybe nobody picks up. But the cost of *not* calling is the thing Kenji saw in that hospital room, and he never wants to be in that room again.

He has made every call since. Not because he's brave. Because the call is such a small thing. It costs nothing. It takes nothing. It's the smallest possible unit of doing your job, and he has never understood why anyone skips it.

This is why Mira's case works differently with him. He doesn't hear a ghost girl's voice and think "I should take this seriously because I'm a good man." He hears a voice giving him information, and he follows it, because that's what you do. The source is unusual but the procedure is the same.

### KENJI AND MIRA — THE DYNAMIC

Kenji does not, at any point, think of Mira as "just a kid."

He doesn't filter her information through her age. He doesn't apply a credibility discount because she's nine. When she gives him a lead, he follows it with the same rigor he'd apply to a tip from a senior officer or a forensic report. Not because he's making a conscious choice to be fair — because he treats all information the same way. It either checks out or it doesn't. The age, background, reputation, and aliveness of the source are not variables in his process.

This is what Mira has never experienced. Not being believed *despite* being a child. Being treated as though the "child" part simply isn't relevant to the quality of the information.

The first time she gives him a lead and he follows it without questioning the source, she waits. She's waiting for the caveat — the "but you're sure?" or the slight change in tone that means the adult has reclassified her input as "needs verification." It doesn't come. He just dials the number. She doesn't comment. She files it as data.

By the fifth or sixth time, she is dealing with the unfamiliar experience of being taken at face value, and she doesn't know what to do with the energy she usually spends on proving she's credible. For the first time in her life — and death — she can focus entirely on the work instead of on convincing someone the work is worth doing.

This is what Kenji gives her. Not warmth. Not sympathy. Infrastructure. He is the system that should have existed around her — the functional chain between "a child observes something" and "an adult acts on it." He completes the circuit that every other adult broke.

### THE RECOGNITION — "SHE DID THE WORK"

Kenji's realization arrives the way things arrive for him — through the work, mid-process, noticed the way he notices changes on his evening walk.

Mid-to-late game. He's cross-referencing leads, building the case map. The silver car sightings — Mira flagged those. The timing pattern around the school — Mira tracked that. The behavioral data on Yui's absences — Mira correlated those with specific days. The location patterns, the recurring contacts, the thread that connects Doi to the florist to the community center — she saw all of it.

She did eighty percent of the work. The observation, the tracking, the pattern recognition — she did all the parts a nine-year-old can do. She couldn't make the calls. She couldn't flash a badge. She couldn't compel anyone to answer. But everything up to that line, she handled. He's been finishing a case she started, not building one from scratch.

He doesn't react with surprise. He's seen enough cases to know that good detective work doesn't require a badge — it requires attention, and Mira paid more attention than most professionals he's worked with. What he feels is recognition. The grim satisfaction of a veteran looking at a junior's fieldwork and finding it sound.

"You did the legwork."

He says it the way he'd say it to a colleague. Matter-of-fact. Professional.

"The car, the timing, the pattern on the school routes. You had it mapped."

Mira: "I know."

"You couldn't make the calls."

"No."

"That's the only piece you were missing."

"I know that too."

A beat. He lights the cigarette he's been keeping behind his ear — the first time in the game he breaks the streak. Takes one drag. Puts it out. Returns it to its position.

"Alright. Let's finish it."

No speech. No sentiment. Two professionals with a case to close, one of whom happens to be nine and dead. The player feels the weight through what he doesn't say: he doesn't praise her. He doesn't marvel. He treats her work the way he treats all good work — as the expected output of someone competent. And for Mira, who has spent her entire existence being told she's a problem, being treated as competent by someone qualified to judge is worth more than any compliment.

She's quiet for a moment. When she comes back, her voice is slightly different. Not softer. Steadier. Like something settled into place.

"...okay."

The player hears: she just got promoted. Not formally. But in the only way that matters.

### WHAT KENJI DOESN'T DO

He doesn't comfort. He says things like "you don't have to apologize" and "what's the next number." These aren't cold. They're the responses of a man who learned that what people need from a detective isn't sympathy — it's continuity.

He doesn't explain himself. He doesn't tell Mira why he follows through. He just makes the calls.

He doesn't see himself clearly. He thinks he's adequate. He'd say: "I just make the calls. That's the job. I don't know why everyone else doesn't."

### THE ARC

**Early game:** Professional distance. Treats the ghost call as an anomalous lead. Does not believe in ghosts. Follows the leads because the leads are good. Interactions are courteous, efficient, devoid of warmth.

**Mid game:** The professional frame expands. Starts asking Mira's opinion. Defers to her reads when they contradict his logic. Starts treating her as a colleague. The recognition scene happens here — the moment he sees the shape of her work in the case.

**Late game:** Fully committed. Burns professional capital. Compresses timelines. The man who walks the same route every night starts moving faster. He's racing her fading and won't name it.

When she tells him about the final call, he is quiet for a long time.

"...alright."

One word. It costs him more than anything else in the game.

After the line goes dead, he sits with a phone that doesn't work anymore. He puts it in the drawer with the others. Five dead phones. Now six. He pours canned coffee into the mug he doesn't usually use. He opens the case file. He finishes the work.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

He doesn't know why everyone else doesn't just make the call. He's never understood that. He probably never will.

## RINA NISHIZAWA — The Social Filter

**Age:** 10 (classmate, one year older than Mira)
**Role:** Reputation engineer / red herring / structural violence made personal

Rina is not the villain. She is something the story needs more than a villain: she is the mechanism by which truth becomes socially non-credible. She didn't destroy Mira. She made it easier for everyone else to dismiss her — and she did it without malice, without a plan, and without ever needing to lie.

### THE CHILDHOOD SEED

Mira and Rina were friends once — briefly, lopsidedly, the way children are when one needs the friendship more than the other. They played together in second grade. Shared books. Mira lent Rina a small notebook with a patterned cover. It never came back.

What actually happened is ambiguous by design. Two versions exist and the game never fully confirms either:

*Version A:* Rina took it, lost it, denied having it. A small theft that became a small lie that became a template.

*Version B:* The notebook was misplaced. Mira accused Rina. The teacher, already familiar with "Mira's accusations," sided with Rina by default. Rina learned — not consciously, not strategically, but with the fluency children absorb social physics — that Mira's version of events could be overruled without effort.

Either way, the outcome is identical: the system rewarded Mira being dismissed. And Rina absorbed that lesson the way a plant absorbs light — automatically, structurally, without deciding to.

### THE DIVERGENCE

By fourth grade, the distance between them is a social fact. Rina is popular — not aggressively, not performatively, but with the quiet centrality of a child who understands group dynamics intuitively. She knows which friendships to maintain, which opinions to echo, which silences to hold.

Mira is isolated. Not because Rina campaigns against her — that would be too visible. Instead, Rina does something far more effective: she performs ambiguity.

- "I think Mira just... misunderstands things sometimes."
- "She always takes things so seriously."
- "We were friends, but she got kind of intense."
- "I'm not saying she's lying. I'm just saying."

Each line is plausible. Each line is socially safe. Each line gives the listener permission to not investigate further. Rina doesn't need to accuse Mira of lying. She only needs to hesitate at the right moment, and the social machinery does the rest.

She is the first person who successfully converted Mira's truth into social fiction. Endo exploits a community. Rina built the conditions that made exploitation frictionless.

### WHAT RINA IS NOT

Rina is not secretly involved in Endo's crimes. She is not directly abusive. She is not the "evil popular girl" archetype. Making her any of these things would collapse the story's strongest idea: that structural harm doesn't require intent. Rina is socially normal, emotionally defensive, indirectly harmful, and internally self-justified. That is far more unsettling than cruelty.

### HOW SHE FUNCTIONS IN THE ECOSYSTEM

*With Kaito:* She would have labeled him "weird" or "creepy" without thinking about it. Her social gravity reinforces his isolation, which reinforces his silence.

*With Aizawa:* Rina benefits from procedural passivity. She never contradicts authority. She is the student teachers point to when they need evidence that the classroom is fine and Mira is the outlier.

*With Reiko:* Rina's version of Mira — "intense," "dramatic," "misunderstands things" — echoes what Reiko already fears about her daughter. It gives Reiko's denial external validation.

*With Endo:* Unknowingly, critically, Rina's reputation work becomes part of the public myth Endo relies on. His framing of Mira doesn't need to be built from scratch. Rina already laid the foundation years ago. She has no idea. That's the point.

### MIRA'S SOUL READ ON RINA

Mira is unusually quiet after this call. Longer than normal.

Then, carefully: "She remembers things wrong on purpose... but not because she's lying. Because it works better if she does."

Pause.

"I used to think she was my friend."

Not anger. Not betrayal. Realization — the kind that arrives years too late and settles like sediment. This is one of the reads where Mira was partially wrong while alive. She thought Rina was being deliberately cruel. The truth is worse: Rina wasn't being anything deliberately. She was just being normal in a system where normal meant Mira disappeared.

### MIRA'S FALLIBILITY (CRITICAL)

Rina is where Mira's imperfection matters most. Mira misread Rina — saw malice where there was social reflex. This makes Mira human and creates real interpretive tension for the player. Mira's read on Rina is emotionally charged and partially compromised. The player has to decide how much weight to give it — which mirrors exactly the decision every adult in Mira's life faced.

### RED HERRING FUNCTION

Multiple suspicion paths, all plausible, none complete:

- *Personal motive:* Childhood resentment. The notebook incident. A relationship that curdled.
- *Framing complicity:* Rina helped build the "liar" reputation Endo later weaponized. The causal chain is visible.
- *Emotional guilt:* She didn't kill Mira. But she made it easier for everyone to ignore her.

The player who chases Rina too hard misses time on other threads. The player who dismisses her too quickly misses key reputation evidence.

### SUPPRESSION AND BREAK

*Suppression:* Social confidence. Controlled tone. Deflection disguised as reasonableness. She talks about Mira the way adults talk about problems they've decided are solved.

*The break:* Not through pressure. Through contradiction — the player presents evidence that doesn't match her curated version. She doesn't collapse. She recalibrates, then stalls.

"...we were kids."

Player presses.

"Everyone said things about her."

Player presents the contradiction.

"...I didn't start it."

Silence.

"...I just didn't stop it."

Then, quieter — almost to herself:

"...it was easier if it was her."

She doesn't cry. She doesn't apologize. She recognizes, for the first time, that "normal" had a body count. Whether she sits with that or buries it again is left ambiguous. The game doesn't give the player a redemption arc for Rina. It gives them a moment of clarity.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

She didn't lie about Mira. She just made it easier for everyone to believe the wrong version.

## KAITO NISHIMURA — The Misread Observer

**Age:** 17
**Role:** Social outcast / silent witness / the strongest red herring in the game

Kaito is not dangerous. He is someone who doesn't perform "normal" well enough to be trusted. He avoids eye contact. He speaks in fragments. He lingers in places too long and responds to social cues on a delay that makes people uncomfortable. Adults describe him as "that kid who hangs around." Other teenagers use the word "creepy" without examining what they mean by it.

He is completely innocent — and structurally, he is the most suspicious person in the case.

### FIRST IMPRESSION

The player encounters Kaito through witness statements before calling him. He was seen near the river, near the school. He was seen "arguing" with Mira — a neighbor who watched from a window and interpreted two awkward people talking with intensity as conflict.

He has no alibi. Not because he was doing something wrong, but because "I was walking around by myself" makes detectives lean forward.

And then there are the maps. Detailed, meticulous sketches of the neighborhood — tunnels, paths, drainage routes, blind spots. To an investigator, these look like planning documents. To Kaito, they are the thing he does instead of socializing.

The player will look at these maps and think: *this is the guy.*

He isn't.

### THE CONNECTION TO MIRA

Not friends. Something harder to name — two people whose isolation occasionally overlapped. They talked sometimes at the river, after school. Their conversations were awkward, tangential, and completely without social performance. Mira would state an observation. Kaito would consider it longer than most people would. Sometimes he'd disagree. She found this refreshing.

They shared interests in small, specific things — urban geography, hidden paths, local folklore about the river. Their dynamic was not warmth. It was the absence of hostility, which for both of them was rare enough to be valuable.

Kaito is one of the only people who didn't dismiss Mira outright. He believed her about the car, about Yui, about most things. But he didn't help her. He didn't stand next to her and say "she's right." He chose silence — because he'd calculated, with the grim arithmetic of a teenager who has been misread his entire life, that his voice would make things worse.

"If I said it, they'd just think I was like her."

### WHAT KAITO KNEW

Mira told him about the silver car. He believed her. He started tracking it — rough times, partial locations, incomplete notes in the margins of his maps. Timestamps that corroborate Mira's claims. Location patterns that narrow the car's routine.

He didn't report any of it. Not because he didn't care. Because "they don't listen" is not paranoia for Kaito — it's data.

### THE INTERROGATION

Calling Kaito should feel uncomfortable, not tense. He is slow. He processes questions on a delay. He gives answers that are technically complete but feel incomplete.

"Why were you at the river?"
"...I go there."
"Why?"
"...it's quiet."

If the player pressures aggressively, Kaito shuts down. Repeats "I didn't do anything" with flat exhaustion. The player sits in the silence they created.

If the player backs off — uses patience, silence, or redirect — Kaito opens in pieces. The absence of aggression is so unusual that he doesn't know how to maintain his guard against it.

The player who treats Kaito the way the system treated Mira — dismissing him because he seems wrong — reproduces the exact failure the game is about. The player who listens gets evidence. The game never announces this.

### MIRA'S SOUL READ ON KAITO

Simple. Honest. One of her shortest.

"He's... not hiding anything. He just doesn't know how to show it properly."

Later: "People think he's scary because he doesn't react right. But he reacts. Just... late."

### THE BREAK

Not from pressure. From trust.

"...she told me about the car."

Pause.

"I saw it too."

Silence.

"I wrote down when it came."

"...I didn't tell anyone."

"They don't listen."

Mira, softly: "...you could've told me to keep trying."

Kaito freezes.

"...I thought you already knew."

His voice cracks. Barely.

"...you always knew things."

Pause.

"I thought you didn't need help."

That last line is the most devastating single sentence in the game. It reframes Mira's competence as the thing that isolated her most completely. She was so good at being right that the one person who believed her assumed she had it handled. Her strength became the reason no one stood beside her.

Mira doesn't respond immediately. When she does: "...okay."

She doesn't say it's fine. She doesn't forgive him. She acknowledges it and moves on.

### RED HERRING TIMING

*Early game:* Kaito surfaces through witness reports. Maps discovered. The player builds a theory.
*Mid game:* Evidence revealed as observation, not planning. Residual suspicion may remain.
*Late game:* Kaito's notes become critical evidence. The person the player suspected breaks the case open.

### WHAT KAITO REPRESENTS

What Mira could have become if she'd lived longer and learned to stop talking. The logical endpoint of her experience: a person who sees everything, reports nothing, and watches the world confirm that silence was the correct strategy.

The tragedy is not that he failed Mira. It's that his failure was rational.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

He didn't speak up — not because he didn't know, but because he already knew what would happen if he did.

## EMI AIZAWA — The Procedural Failure

**Age:** 27
**Role:** After-school tutor / assistant teacher at Yanagi Municipal Elementary
**Tenure:** 2–3 years at the school

Aizawa is not dangerous because she's unstable. She is dangerous because she stopped acting at the exact moment it mattered — and she stopped because the system taught her, through direct experience, that acting is punished.

She is soft-spoken, visibly tired, and overly structured. Her lesson plans are immaculate. Her desk is organized with geometric precision. She sanitizes her hands constantly — between conversations, between tasks, in the middle of sentences. Not anxiety. Ritual. The observable behavior of a person who has decided that if she can control the small things perfectly, the large things she can't control will hurt less.

Students perform well under her. Parents like her. Administration considers her reliable. She is, by every institutional metric, an excellent teacher.

She is also the person who believed Mira, documented everything Mira said, and chose to do nothing further.

### THE BACKSTORY

First year, different school — Aizawa reported suspected abuse. Escalated aggressively. Contacted child services directly. She was certain. She was also correct.

It didn't matter. The parents threatened legal action. Administration issued a formal reprimand. Her supervisor told her: "You're young. You'll learn where the lines are. Focus on teaching."

She learned. The lesson: seeing something is not the same as being allowed to act. Being right does not protect you. The system does not reward intervention — it rewards documentation. Write it down. File it. Let the process work. If the process doesn't work, that's the process's failure, not yours.

She carried this lesson to Yanagi like scar tissue.

### THE CONNECTION TO MIRA

Mira reported Yui's situation to Aizawa directly — because Aizawa seemed least likely to dismiss her. Mira had good instincts. This was one of the times her instincts were exactly right and it made no difference.

Aizawa believed her. Immediately. Documented everything. Date, time, exact wording. Filed it according to protocol. Flagged it in the system.

She did not follow up. She did not contact child services. She did not call Yui's home. She followed the procedure she'd been trained to follow after the last time she broke it — and the procedure was designed to absorb information without producing action.

She knew this. She chose it anyway.

### WHAT AIZAWA IS NOT

Not secretly violent. Not "about to snap." Not a suspect in any meaningful sense. Making her dangerous would collapse the story's point. Her function is to be the system working exactly as designed — absorbing truth, filing it, producing no response. She is proof that the institution functioned correctly and a child still died.

### FIRST IMPRESSION

Polite, cooperative, carefully structured. She answers with precision — not for the detective, but for herself. She's been waiting for this call since Mira's body was found.

The off-feeling is subtle. She pauses too long before answering questions about specific students' home lives. She deflects anything that moves from "what she observed" to "what she did about it."

Mira's Soul Read: "She feels like she's holding something heavy very carefully... like if she drops it, it breaks everything."

### THE INTERROGATION

Aizawa resists through politeness. She becomes more organized under pressure, not less. More precise. Shorter sentences. She's been pressured by people with more authority than Kenji and survived by becoming smaller and more correct.

The player who uses patience — asking about her teaching philosophy, her career before Yanagi — will find the seams. She'll reveal the shape of what she lost.

The break comes through moral contradiction. Her own records. The proof that she saw, believed, and filed.

"Do you know what happens when you're wrong about something like that?"

Silence.

"They don't thank you for trying."

Pause.

"You don't get praised. You get written up."

Long silence.

"...so I wrote it down instead."

Mira, quietly: "...that didn't help."

"I know."

Then:

"She wasn't wrong. I knew she wasn't wrong. I documented every word she said. I followed procedure."

Her voice thinning:

"I chose the version of my job that let me sleep."

She doesn't cry. That restraint is what makes it devastating. The player sits in the silence of a person who built a functioning life around a decision she cannot undo and will not forgive herself for.

### THE PHYSICAL DETAIL

The hand-sanitizing. The paper-straightening. Mira notices: "She keeps fixing things that aren't broken."

After the break scene, it recontextualizes. She wasn't fixing things. She was performing control over the only territory she had left.

### HOW SHE FITS THE ECOSYSTEM

*With Mira:* The failure that hurts most — it came closest to working. Mira chose her correctly. The connection was real. But the system had already taught Aizawa that connection without institutional backing is just personal risk.

*With Rina:* Both reinforce dismissal from different angles. Rina socially. Aizawa procedurally. They never coordinated. The system is self-reinforcing.

*With Kaito:* Inverted mirrors. Both saw the truth. Kaito stayed silent. Aizawa documented — a more sophisticated version of the same silence.

*With Endo:* Aizawa's documentation is useful to him. The school records showing "Mira Kitahara — behavioral concerns, multiple reports" reinforces his narrative. She contributed to the paper trail he weaponizes. She has no idea.

*With Reiko:* They failed from opposite directions. Reiko didn't believe. Aizawa believed and didn't act. One chose denial. The other chose delegation. Mira fell through the gap between them.

### SUSPICION TIMING

Mid-game peak — after Kaito's initial suspicion has been partially resolved, before Endo's pattern becomes visible. Aizawa is the pivot point where the player transitions from suspecting individuals to suspecting the system.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

She didn't fail because she didn't care. She failed because she learned — correctly — that caring is punished.

## REIKO KITAHARA — The Mother

**Age:** 34
**Role:** Grieving parent / emotional anchor / the failure that hurts most personally

Reiko is not a villain. She is a woman who loved her daughter and did not understand her, and she is now living in the space between those two facts.

### THE PERSON

Night-shift nurse — competent, exhausted, stretched across too many hours. She didn't disbelieve Mira out of malice. She disbelieved her because believing Mira meant the world was more dangerous than Reiko had the energy to confront. Every accusation Mira made was another emergency Reiko couldn't afford. So she chose, repeatedly, to interpret Mira's observations as imagination, drama, a phase.

She chose wrong. She knows this now.

### CORE CONFLICT

She loved Mira. She did not believe her. The game never resolves this contradiction because it cannot be resolved. Both things are true. Neither cancels the other.

**Core fear:** "I failed her in a way that can't be undone."
**Core desire:** "To believe she was a good mother."
**Suppression:** Composure. Politeness. "Functioning adult mode." The rehearsed quality of someone who has prepared for police questions the way she prepares for a shift — professionally.

### IN THE GAME

Reiko is the first call. She is polished, rehearsed, and heartbreaking. She has prepared herself for this conversation. Underneath, she is collapsing.

Mira's Soul Read of her own mother is the most painful moment in Chapter 1 — Mira is clinical about it, almost too precise, and then goes quiet for the rest of the session.

### THE BREAK SCENE

After reading Mira's observation notebook — recovered late in the game. She's quiet at first. Too quiet.

"...she wrote it down."

"...with dates."

She flips a page. Hands shaking.

"I remember this day."

Pause.

"She told me this."

Long silence.

"I told her she was imagining things."

She laughs once. Sharp. Wrong.

"I said that."

Her voice breaks:

"I said that to my child."

She tries to stay composed.

"I thought — if I didn't believe it — then it wasn't real."

Now crying — but trying not to.

"What kind of logic is that?"

Silence.

"...the kind that lets you sleep."

### REIKO IS NOT THE KILLER

But her negligence is part of how the killer operated. The game never lets her or the player forget that.

### THE FRAMING CRISIS

Mid-late game, Reiko is shown the fabricated evidence connecting Mira to Sora's disappearance. She almost believes it. This is the cruelest moment — not because Reiko is weak, but because the lie is built on real materials. Mira really was difficult. She really did make accusations. The fabrication just points all those true things in a false direction.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

"If I didn't believe it, it wasn't real." — the kind of logic that lets you sleep.

## YUI SAKAMOTO — The Living Parallel

**Age:** 11
**Role:** Potential victim / living proof Mira was right

Yui is everything Mira wasn't: quiet, agreeable, invisible by choice. She makes herself small so that the large things in her life have room to move without collision. She lives with her mother and her mother's boyfriend, Takeshi, and she has learned the architecture of avoidance — which rooms, which hours, which topics produce silence and which produce sound.

She was Mira's only friend. This is not widely known because Yui managed the association carefully — meeting Mira at the river after school, where nobody from class would see them. She feels enormous guilt about this.

### CORE DYNAMICS

**Core fear:** "If I say it, it gets worse."
**Core desire:** "Someone to fix it without me asking."
**Suppression:** Compliance. Soft voice. Avoidance.

### WHAT MIRA KNEW

Mira knew about Takeshi. She tried to tell a teacher. The teacher called Yui's mother. Yui's mother said Mira was making things up. Mira stopped going to that teacher's class for a week.

### IN THE GAME

Yui is discovered through Mira's old contacts. Calling her is one of the first major branches. She is scared, lying to protect her mother, and in real danger. The player must navigate her trust carefully — push too hard and she shuts down; wait too long and Takeshi escalates.

**Saving Yui is not optional for the true ending.** But it requires Kenji to act on Mira's word alone — the same leap of faith every adult in Mira's life refused to take.

### THE BREAK

After enough trust:

"...can I ask something?"

"If I tell you something... you won't call my mom first, right?"

That line alone tells the player everything.

Later:

"Mira told someone."

Silence.

"...it got worse after that."

Crying quietly.

"I don't want it to get worse again."

### MIRA'S SOUL READ ON YUI

"She's... small. Not her body. Everything else. Like she's been packing herself into a box and she's gotten very good at it."

A long pause.

"She was my friend and I didn't fix it. I need you to fix it."

This is the first time Mira asks for something without pretending she doesn't care.

### TAKESHI KANO — Yui's Stepfather

**Age:** 41. Mid-level office worker with a short temper and a history of relationships that end the same way. Physically imposing, superficially polite, lies with fluency.

Mira's read: "Cold. Not angry-cold. Organized-cold. Like a freezer with everything in labeled boxes."

**Takeshi is arrested in every route.** The game does not allow a version where Yui stays. But when it happens, and how much damage occurs first, depends on the player.

### YUI'S MOTHER

**Age:** 35. Survival-focused, emotionally constrained. Parallel to Reiko — a different kind of mother making a different kind of wrong choice.

Core line: "Do you know how hard it is to start over with a child?"

A background character, not a full NPC — but her presence reinforces the theme: adults choosing the version of reality they can survive.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

"You won't call my mom first... right?" — the question that tells the player everything about what the system did to the children it was supposed to protect.

## SHUNSUKE DOI — The False Confession

**Age:** 62
**Role:** Red herring / community fixture / tragic figure / the game's first lesson about listening

Doi runs the Yanagi Mart, a cramped convenience store three blocks from the elementary school. He is the man Mira reported for "watching children." He was watching children — specifically his grandson, who attends the school and whose custody situation prevents anything closer than observation from across a street.

He is gruff, inarticulate, and visibly uncomfortable talking to police. He looks guilty. He sounds guilty.

### IN THE GAME

Doi is a deliberate misdirect. Everything circumstantial points his way. The player can pursue this thread aggressively — and Doi will confess to things he didn't do.

Not because he's guilty. Because he's been expecting to be accused of something for years and he's tired of waiting. His confession is surrender, not guilt.

Breaking through the false confession requires empathy, not pressure. It's the first time the game teaches the player that Mira's ability doesn't replace detective work — it redirects it.

### MIRA'S SOUL READ

"Sadness so old it's turned into furniture — it's just part of the room now."

### THE PAYOFF

Late in the game, Doi provides a crucial detail about the silver car. He saw it too. He didn't report it because the last time he talked to police about children near a school, it went badly.

**Core line:** "I stopped reporting things because the last time I did, they took my grandson."

The system that failed Mira also failed him.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

He confessed because being guilty felt easier than being doubted.

## HARUKI ISE — The Guilty Ally

**Age:** 29
**Role:** Guilt carrier / field ally / moral mirror

Haruki is Mira's former homeroom teacher — the one who coined "disruptive honesty." Young, earnest, haunted by the memory of Mira saying "Mr. Ise, Yui's mom's boyfriend hits her" and Haruki responding by calling Yui's mother first.

He followed protocol. Protocol was wrong.

### PERSONALITY

Earnest to the point of discomfort. Talks too fast when nervous. Compulsive need to be helpful that masks genuine moral distress. The kind of person who became a teacher because he believed in the system and is now watching it digest a child it was supposed to protect.

### IN THE GAME

First adult who voluntarily contacts Kenji — calls the precinct, asks for the detective on the Kitahara case, says "I think I should have done something differently." Enormously useful and enormously suspicious.

Mira's Soul Read: "He's... loud inside. Like a room where every radio is on a different station. He feels guilty but not in a hiding way — in a drowning way. He wants someone to tell him it wasn't his fault." Pause. "It was partially his fault."

### FUNCTION

Bridge between system and individuals. Has access to school records, parent contacts, community trust. Also a liability — his guilt makes him reckless. He wants redemption badly enough to endanger the investigation by acting without Kenji's coordination.

The player manages Haruki by deciding how much to trust him with. Give him too much and he tips off the wrong person. Give him too little and he goes looking on his own. The sweet spot is treating him like what he is: a decent person who made a mistake and needs direction, not absolution.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

He did what was "correct" and it caused harm. He's been living in that gap ever since.

## FUMIKO ARAI — The Observer of Patterns

**Age:** 44
**Role:** Information broker / wildcard / thematic counterpoint

Fumiko writes for the Yanagi Ward community paper — a four-page weekly that covers school events and local business openings and, when she can convince her editor, actual news. Fifteen years ago, she was a reporter at a national paper. Fifteen years ago, she was right about a story her editors killed. She's been at the community paper since.

### PERSONALITY

Sharp, impatient, operates on coffee and professional resentment. Treats every conversation like a negotiation. Will trade information for information. Cannot be charmed, flattered, or intimidated. Dark sense of humor. Genuine moral compass disguised as cynicism because sincerity hasn't worked out for her professionally.

### IN THE GAME

Has sources Kenji doesn't — embedded in the neighborhood for years. Knows which families have secrets, which businesses have debts, which community figures have influence they shouldn't. Has information about a previous child disappearance in the ward from eight years ago that was ruled a runaway case.

Mira's read: "She's tired the same way you're tired. But hers is angry-tired. She still thinks she can change something. I like her."

Fumiko is the player's access to the larger pattern — the historical cases that reveal Endo's eleven-year cycle. She is also the character most likely to publish something that blows the investigation open prematurely if the player doesn't manage the relationship.

### FUNCTION

Reveals: this isn't the first time. Provides the historical context that transforms a single murder into a systemic pattern. Her files, combined with Kaito's maps and Doi's observations, form the evidentiary backbone of the case against Endo.

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

She was right about a story once and it cost her everything. She hasn't stopped being right. She's just stopped expecting anyone to care.

## SORA HAYASHI — The Missing Child

**Age:** 8
**Role:** The living stakes / rescue objective / Mira's legacy

Sora is alive. The player does not know this for certain until the final act, but the game plants enough ambiguity to sustain hope. He is a quiet, imaginative boy who draws elaborate maps of imaginary cities in his notebook and was last seen walking home from a piano lesson he was already three weeks behind on.

### IN THE GAME

Sora is not a character the player interacts with until the very end. He is a presence — discussed, searched for, theorized about. His absence is the engine of Act III's urgency.

### THE FINAL CALL

Mira reaches him. The phone rings in a place it shouldn't. A child picks up.

"Someone is coming. His name is Kenji. He's grumpy but he listens. I need you to be brave for about twenty more minutes. Can you do that?"

Sora says yes.

"My name is Mira. Please remember it."

### EPILOGUE

Six months later. New school. Class assignment: write about someone important. Sora writes about a girl named Mira who called him when he was scared and told him to be brave.

His teacher asks who she was.

"She was the one who called."

### ONE-LINE SUMMARY

He remembers her name. That is what she wanted. She just couldn't say it.
