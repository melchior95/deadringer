This document maps the information economy of the investigation: who the player can reach, what they know, what unlocks what, and where each character's key scenes land. The character bible defines WHO. This document defines WHEN and in WHAT ORDER.

## THE YANAGI PHONE PHENOMENON

Yanagi's phone infrastructure is half-alive. The old exchange was partially decommissioned but never fully disconnected. Endo built his surveillance system on top of it. The side effect: the infrastructure bleeds into daily life. Calls route wrong. Static has structure. Lines echo. Wrong numbers connect to the right people. The town has lived with this for years and normalized it — the way a community normalizes any persistent strangeness that never quite rises to the level of a problem.

Kenji, the outsider, is the one who notices it isn't normal.

### Folklore Layer

Different residents describe the phenomenon differently. No single official name — a shared understanding expressed through local folklore:

| Who | What They Say | Chapter |
|-----|--------------|---------|
| Doi | "Phones here have always been funny. My grandmother used to say the lines have long memories." | Ch 3 |
| Contact during Yui/Rina investigation | "You calling from Yanagi? The reception out there... my cousin says her calls cross all the time" | Ch 4 |
| School office contact | "The phones in Yanagi have long memories" (after wrong connection) | Ch 5 |
| Mira | "The static sounds different here. It's... layered" | Ch 5 |
| Fumiko | "I have a file of phone anomalies in this district going back eight years. The telecom company inspected twice. Said the infrastructure was 'normal, just old.' I never published — it wasn't a story. Just a pattern I couldn't explain" | Ch 6 |
| Haruki | "The students dare each other to call the bridge number at night. One of them told me she heard someone breathing. I told her it was wind in the cables" | Ch 7 |
| Background legend | Years ago, a woman claimed she received a call from her dead husband. The call originated within Yanagi. She was told she imagined it. She didn't. The lines were carrying residual signals — the same mechanism that allows Mira to call Kenji. | Ambient |

The town phenomenon predates Mira. She is not the first signal to push through. She is the first one who reached someone who listened.

### Escalation Curve

| Phase | Chapters | Player Experience | What's Actually Happening |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Local color** | Ch 1-3 | Bridge number static. Doi mentions phone weirdness. Kenji writes it off as bad rural infrastructure | The exchange exists. It has always been bleeding |
| **Unsettling** | Ch 4-5 | Micro-Incident 1: call routes to wrong person, but the wrong person is relevant. A local shrugs: "Yanagi phones do that." Mira: "The static sounds different here. It's layered" | Endo is monitoring. The investigation generates activity on the exchange |
| **The Pivot** | Ch 7 | Micro-Incident 2: breathing on the line. A child. Alive. Present tense. Mira: "That wasn't me. That was someone alive." | Sora is near an active cable run. His fear generates the same clarity-signal Mira generated |
| **Active** | Ch 8-9 | Calls bleed into each other. Echoes during NPC conversations. The phones feel watched | Endo is monitoring harder. His increased activity creates more bleed-through |
| **Revelatory** | Ch 10-12 | The exchange room. Everything explains itself. The town's "funny phones" were surveillance architecture | Full reveal. Mira amplified. The other children heard |

### Design Principle

The escalation is reactive, not random. As Kenji investigates harder, Endo monitors more through the exchange, which causes more infrastructure bleed-through, which produces more phone weirdness. The player should feel that the town is responding to their investigation. That is the present-tense momentum engine.

## ACT I: THE FIRST CALL (Chapters 1–3)

### Chapter 1 — 3:47 AM

**Player knows:** Nothing. A dead girl. An open case going cold.

**The call:** Mira reaches Kenji. Matter-of-fact, slightly annoyed, no explanation for the supernatural mechanism. "I have notes." Partnership begins as professional skepticism — Kenji files her as an unusual source, not a ghost.

**Mechanics introduced:**
- Phone as primary interface
- Soul Read (Mira reads Kenji's environment, not people yet — establishing the sensory vocabulary)
- Notebook system — player begins logging

**Mira's register:** Full armor. "Detective." Clinical delivery. Testing him in small ways he doesn't notice.

**Ends with:** Kenji has Mira on the line and a case file that stopped moving. She gives him a place to start — Yanagi.

---

### Chapter 2 — The Yanagi District

**Player knows:** Mira's name, cause of death, the case's stalled state.

**Physical investigation:** Kenji walks the Yanagi district. First-person scene exploration. The neighborhood is quiet, maintained, ordinary in a way that feels curated.

**Evidence collected:**
- Reiko's phone number (from case file / scene)
- Doi's phone number (corner store, community board)
- Bridge number — scratched into concrete under a bridge. Old. Unidentified.
- *Sora artifact (unrecognized):* A bench near Doi's store. Faint colored pencil marks on the seat slats. A discarded sheet of graph paper in a nearby bin — a fragment of a city map, precise, unsigned. The player can log it. It means nothing yet.

**Mira's commentary:** Dry observations about the neighborhood. She knows this place. She doesn't say "I lived here" — she says "the vending machine on the corner has been broken since April" and lets Kenji do the math.

**Ends with:** Three numbers in the call log. The investigation has contacts.

---

### Chapter 3 — First Calls

**Player knows:** The neighborhood layout, three phone numbers, Mira's observational style.

**Call 1 — Reiko:** Rehearsed, heartbreaking. She has answered these questions before. Her grief is real and her account is edited — not lying, but performing the version of motherhood she can survive. Mira listens. Mira's first Soul Read on a person: the most painful read in the game. The player gets emotional weather — pressure, fracture, love shaped wrong. **Memory fragment trigger:** After the call, the player experiences the Reiko Denial memory — Mira's POV, standing in her mother's kitchen, reporting the man watching the school, watching Reiko's face close. Interactive, not cutscene. The player feels the specific helplessness of delivering correct information to someone who won't act on it.

**Call 2 — Doi:** Defensive, suspicious. A man who has been looked at sideways since Mira's reports named him. Gruff. Unhelpful on the surface. Mentions offhand: "The boy who used to sit outside my store after school, drawing. He hasn't been around." Not a report — a gap in his routine. *Sora artifact #2, unrecognized.*

**Call 3 — Bridge number:** No ring. No voice. No recording. A sound anomaly — static with structure. Patterned, layered, rhythmic. Most people would dismiss it as a dead line. The player can log the anomaly. It sits in the notebook as a question mark. First hint that Yanagi's phone infrastructure is abnormal.

**Mira mentions Yui:** Not by name. "There's someone. A girl. She needs... she's in a situation." Reluctant. She doesn't want to expose Yui's secret. She also can't leave her in that house. The conflict is real.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Reiko is hiding something (grief, not guilt). Doi is defensive (reputation, not crime). A strange number connects to nothing useful. And Mira has a person she's protecting.

## ACT II: THE WEB (Chapters 4–7)

### Chapter 4 — Yui

**Player knows:** Mira has someone she needs to protect. Reiko and Doi are contactable but not fully open.

**The Yui branch:** Mira discloses enough for Kenji to make contact. Calling Yui is the game's first major mechanical challenge — she is trained to protect the lie. The intent system matters here: Pressure shuts her down. Reassure is too slow. Silence works — Yui fills the space with fragments that reveal more than direct answers would. **Yui's folding is audible from her first call** — the player hears soft, precise paper creasing beneath her performed composure. The folding is her self-regulation mechanism; its rhythm maps her emotional state (see character bible: FOLDING TELL SYSTEM).

**The choice:** Pursue Yui's safety now (risking the larger case by diverting Kenji's attention and potentially alerting Yui's mother) or continue building evidence (leaving Yui in danger longer). This is the game's first real moral fork.

**If player acts on Yui:** Kenji intervenes. Yui is removed from the house. This triggers Mira's crying scene — the mid-game emotional break. The armor comes off because for the first time, an adult heard Mira and acted. (See character bible: THE SCENE WHERE MIRA CRIES.)

**If player delays:** Yui remains in danger. Mira's tension builds through the chapter — she becomes more insistent, less clinical. The armor slips from anxiety rather than relief. The chapter's emotional driver shifts from breakthrough to dread. The crying scene does not fire here but is NOT skipped — it defers to a later catalyst (see CRYING SCENE PLACEMENT below).

**Baseline investigation (runs regardless of Yui choice):**
- **Rina available:** Through school contacts or Reiko, Rina becomes reachable. Her calls are socially polished, helpful-seeming, subtly corrosive. She provides the community's consensus narrative about Mira — the one that made it easy to dismiss her. **Memory fragment trigger:** The Rina Social Distortion memory — the notebook incident, Mira's POV. The ambiguity of what happened. The community's social physics revealed in miniature: truth becomes negotiable when the source is expendable. The player should log Rina's specific phrasing ("Mira just misunderstands things sometimes") — it will echo in the framing language three chapters later.
- **Kaito's notebooks found:** Neighborhood canvassing for the Yui lead turns up secondary evidence — witness reports mention a boy in the neighborhood who tracked patterns. His notebooks — timestamped logs of the neighborhood's social patterns — are recovered. The player builds a theory: surveillance? Stalking? The notebooks look suspicious. This is the suspicion arc's early-mid phase.
- **Phone phenomenon seed:** During Yui or Rina investigation, a contact mentions Yanagi's phone weirdness offhand — "You calling from Yanagi? The reception out there... my cousin says her calls cross all the time." The player files this as rural infrastructure. It is the first time someone outside Yanagi comments on the town's phone behavior. (See: THE YANAGI PHONE PHENOMENON — Folklore Layer.)

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Yui needs help (acted on or deferred). Mira cares about someone specific. The community had a consensus about Mira. A drawing boy stopped coming to the river and nobody asked why. And someone was watching the neighborhood with uncomfortable precision.

### Chapter 5 — The Teacher's Guilt

**Player knows:** Yui's situation (acted on or deferred). The community's consensus narrative about Mira. Kaito's notebooks raise questions.

**Haruki contacts Kenji.** First adult in the game who voluntarily reaches out. "I think I should have done something differently." He is excessively helpful — offering school records, parent lists, building access. He's trying to demonstrate, to himself, that the procedures existed.

**School records unlock:**
- Three dismissed reports — other children raised concerns about "an adult who was always around." Safety council reviewed and dismissed. The three reports correspond to children who subsequently disappeared.
- *Sora named:* Haruki describes him — "quiet, not a problem, never flagged." Mentions the maps. "He drew these city things in his notebook instead of taking notes sometimes. I never marked him down for it. They were really good." Sora becomes a person.
- *Ogawa personnel file:* A firing, a timeline gap, a name that appears and disappears. Looks like institutional noise. The player can log it or skip it.
- *Endo named:* The safety council committee that reviewed and dismissed the three reports was chaired by Masato Endo. His name appears in the institutional paperwork — not as a suspect, not as a person of interest. As a signature on a form. A committee chair in a filing system. The player may not notice. The name sits in the notebook three chapters before it becomes a voice.

**Aizawa accessible:** Through school records and Haruki's context. Her calls are procedural walls — dates, sequences, filed reports. Getting past the wall requires patience, not pressure. The player who listens to what she's *not* saying learns more than from what she says. **Memory fragment trigger:** The Aizawa Procedure memory — Mira's POV, reporting Yui's abuse. Aizawa documents it, files it, produces no action. The player watches a true report enter the institutional machine and disappear. This is the third and final memory fragment; by now the player has experienced the failure chain from Mira's side three times (mother, peer, institution) and understands the pattern viscerally.

**Kaito's first call:** If the player follows up on the notebooks, Kaito is now reachable. His spatial-temporal annotation is immediately, memorably strange — within thirty seconds the player clocks his speech quirk. Every answer comes with coordinates nobody asked for: "...she was at the bench. The third one. Near the willow, not the metal ones by the path." His annotations sound like surveillance transcripts, reinforcing the red herring. The player who matched his tempo gets more: precise spatial data that will later become critical evidence. (See character bible: KAITO — The Coordinates.)

**Micro-Incident 1 — The Wrong Connection:** During the school records investigation, Kenji dials a follow-up contact — the school office or a number from the dismissed reports. The call connects, but it is not who he called. A woman's voice, mid-sentence, already talking — not to him. To someone else. A conversation already in progress:

"...the reports from that year, I don't think anyone actually read them before they were—"

The line cuts. Dead air. Then normal dial tone. Kenji checks his phone — he dialed correctly. He tries again. This time it connects normally. He mentions the odd connection to the person he reaches. The response, casual, almost bored: "Oh — yeah, the lines out here cross sometimes. Been like that for years. My mother used to say the phones in Yanagi have long memories."

Mira's reaction: quiet for a moment. Then: "The static on that call was different. It had... layers. Like two things playing at once." She does not push it. She files it the way she files everything — as an observation waiting for a frame.

*Late-game payoff:* The fragment was a real conversation — routed through the exchange. A teacher and an administrator discussing Mira's dismissed reports, years ago. The infrastructure recorded it. The "wrong connection" was the system leaking a real signal. The player who revisits this moment after the exchange reveal realizes: the lines were trying to tell Kenji something five chapters before he could hear it.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** The system had warnings and didn't act. Multiple children reported. The reports were absorbed by a specific committee. Something structural is wrong — and it has a chairman's name on it. The town's phones behave oddly — locals explain it as old infrastructure. A new contact (Kaito) talks like a surveillance transcript.

### Chapter 6 — Doi's False Confession

**Player knows:** A pattern of ignored reports. A committee that dismissed everything. Kaito's notebooks still unexplained.

**Community pressure:** The investigation has stirred Yanagi. Ambient suspicion lands on the most obvious target — the gruff loner reported by the dead girl. Doi feels it.

**The confession:** Under pressure (from the community, not necessarily from Kenji), Doi confesses to involvement. He is lying. The player must recognize this through:
- Mira's read (she sees performance, not guilt — "He's... loud inside. Like a room where every radio is on a different station")
- Inconsistencies in his timeline
- The photograph behind his counter (Ren, the grandson he lost custody of)

**Breaking through:** One of the game's emotional peaks. The player who uses Silence during Doi's confession hears it collapse. He stops performing guilt and starts leaking grief. The false confession was an act of surrender — easier to be guilty of something he didn't do than to keep being suspected of it while carrying the weight of what he actually lost.

**Doi's real testimony (unlocked):** The silver car. He saw it. Three times. Always near the school, always at pickup hours. He didn't report it because the last time he reported something (his concern about Ren), the system used it against him. The silver car is real evidence, suppressed by a man the system taught not to speak.

**Fumiko enters:** The community disturbance — Doi's public situation, Haruki's activity, the investigation's ripple effects — draws out a woman who has been watching Yanagi for fourteen years. Kenji finds her through community contacts and the trail of someone who has been asking questions before the detective arrived. She does not call Kenji. She makes him find her. She provides historical context: a pattern of disappearances, a previous case, a child eight years ago described as "difficult" and "prone to fabrication." Among her materials, Fumiko mentions offhand: a file of phone anomalies in the Yanagi district going back eight years. The telecom company inspected twice — said the infrastructure was "normal, just old." She never published it. It wasn't a story. Just a pattern she couldn't explain. The player now has the word "pattern" applied to both disappearances and phone behavior. The word starts to rhyme.

**Haruki's point of no return (branching):** Before his break scene, while still operating on reckless guilt, Haruki pushes a lead without coordinating with Kenji. He visits a family connected to one of the dismissed reports. He means well. He is right about the connection. But the visit alerts people the investigation hasn't reached yet. The player cannot undo this. The consequences cascade into Ch 7 — NPCs may be less cooperative, information may be harder to access, Endo may adjust earlier.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Doi is innocent but damaged. The silver car is a real lead. A journalist has been tracking this pattern for over a decade. Someone the player trusted (Haruki) has acted without coordination and the ripples are spreading.

### Chapter 7 — The Silver Car

**Player knows:** Silver car sightings from multiple sources. Fumiko's historical pattern. Haruki's uncoordinated action has disturbed the web.

**Convergence:** Multiple threads arrive at the same point:
- Doi's suppressed observation (silver car, three sightings)
- Fumiko's old files (pattern of disappearances, historical case)
- Haruki's school records (dismissed reports, the three children)
- Kaito's notebooks (social pattern logs — vehicle that didn't fit the neighborhood's regular pattern)

The silver car is registered to a holding company. The holding company's community liaison is Masato Endo. The name from the committee forms in Ch 5.

**Fumiko's historical case connects:** The child in her folder — eight years ago, "difficult," "prone to fabrication" — matches one of the three school records Haruki surfaced. Two evidence streams converge independently on the same name.

**Fumiko's blind spot (player trap):** Fumiko pushes a lead on a family that moved out of Yanagi — connects them to the disappearance pattern. The timeline fits. The family had a child flagged as "behavioral." Following this lead costs the player time during the chapter where every other thread is paying off. The family left for economic reasons, not because of Endo. Fumiko overfitted. The recovery mechanic is Mira's Soul Read on the wrongly-flagged family: "They're not hiding anything relevant. They're just sad about leaving. It's a different kind of sad." The player who follows this lead learns to trust Fumiko's instincts while verifying her conclusions. The player who skips it never encounters the trap — but also never learns the lesson that matters in the late game.

**Kaito's notebooks recontextualized:** Evidence revealed as observation, not surveillance. His logs are social patterns, not stalking. Residual suspicion may remain, but the shape of his data is clearly civilian, not criminal. His logs become critical evidence — the vehicle that didn't fit the pattern.

**Fumiko's infrastructure map (enters evidence):** Among the documents Fumiko provides during the convergence is a folded city planning document from the municipal archives — telecommunications infrastructure of the Yanagi district, cable runs, junction boxes, the old exchange routes. She obtained it for a community center article. She annotated it in red pen. She doesn't know its significance. It enters the player's evidence as one document among many. It becomes the most important piece of paper in the game three chapters later.

**Ogawa thread deepens (optional):** The player cross-referencing Endo's involvement in school decisions notices he was on the review committee that recommended Ogawa's termination. Same committee that dismissed Mira's reports. Same committee, opposite outcomes.

**The framing surfaces:** Evidence appears connecting Mira to Sora — fabricated chat logs, planted items creating the impression of "a difficult dynamic." The player, who knows Mira by now, recognizes fabrication. The framing's language echoes Rina's: "misunderstands," "difficult," "says things." The player who logged Rina's phrasing in Ch 4 sees it here — the community consensus Rina provided was the precondition for the frame to work. Endo didn't need to invent the "liar" reputation. It was already built. Rina is recontextualized: not a villain, but a mechanism. Among the planted evidence: a page from one of Sora's maps. Genuine. In the lower right corner, a river with a bend, two tiny figures on a bank. *Sora artifact — this one is real among the fabricated.*

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Endo is connected. Not proven — connected. The player has a suspect they can see but can't touch. The community's most helpful man is at the center of every thread.

## ACT III: THE LOCKED ROOM (Chapters 8–10)

### Chapter 8 — Endo

**Player knows:** Endo is connected to the silver car, the holding company, and the committee that dismissed every report. But he is a community pillar with fifteen years of social capital.

**Endo calls Kenji.** He does not wait to be found. He calls. Warm, helpful, offering cooperation. This is the most dangerous call in the game because the player knows (or suspects) and Endo sounds like help.

**Endo as NPC:** Every call produces actionable information. He suggests leads. He provides context. He connects Kenji to people. The leads are real. The information is accurate. He is helping the investigation go in every direction except toward him.

**Mira's read — the locked room:** The silence. The one read she can't finish. She starts and stops. "He's..." Long pause. "I can't get in. It's not like Reiko where there's a door and I can feel what's behind it. This is... there's no door. It's just wall. All the way around."

**The tell (player must discover):** Endo knows things with the wrong resolution. He describes emotional states he shouldn't access. He uses phrasing that echoes other NPCs' private conversations. The helpfulness is the evidence — its *precision* reveals impossible access. The player's task: map the gap between what he should know and what he does know.

**Aizawa's break:** While investigating the school's institutional response — circling back through Endo's committee decisions, his influence on which reports were dismissed — the player returns to Aizawa. If enough trust has been built, her procedural wall cracks. "I chose the version of my job that let me sleep." The sanitizer bottle click. The silence. The player sees the cost of institutional survival. This lands in the same chapter as Endo's seamless composure — the contrast is the point. Aizawa is what institutional pressure looks like when it breaks someone. Endo is what it looks like when someone masters it.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Endo is either the most helpful person in Yanagi or the most dangerous. Mira can't read him. Aizawa broke under the weight of the same system Endo controls. The player has never encountered a wall this complete.

---

### Chapter 9 — The Framing Unravels

**Player knows:** Endo is the suspect. His knowledge pattern is impossible without hidden access. The framing evidence from Ch 7 needs deconstruction.

**Structure note:** This is a free-investigation chapter. Multiple concurrent leads are available — framing evidence, Endo's property, Reiko, the Ogawa thread. The player chooses which to pursue and in what order. The density is intentional: the investigation is accelerating because there are more threads than the player has time for. Items below are available paths, not a linear sequence.

**Evidence examination:** The player deconstructs Endo's planted evidence piece by piece. Each item traces back to Endo's access. The map page is the anomaly — not incriminating, not fabricated. A child's drawing that includes, in one small corner, evidence that Sora noticed the same girl the community chose not to see.

**Sora's map recontextualized:** The player (and Mira, if shown) recognizes the river drawing. The two figures. Sora drew Mira — not because they were friends, but because he noticed her. Endo planted this page without examining it. He used a child's map as a prop and didn't notice it contained a diagram of his own patterns.

**Haruki's break:** While examining the framing documentation, Haruki discovers that his label — "disruptive honesty" — appears in the files Endo used to build the case against Mira's credibility. His phrase. His words. In the file that was used to frame a child he was trying to help. "I named it. I gave it a name and they used the name." The recklessness burns off. What replaces it is colder, more focused, more useful: anger that has found its target.

**The garden:** The garden is street-visible — neighbors mention it, Endo discusses it openly, it wraps around the south side of his home. Kenji can observe it during neighborhood investigation without requiring access to Endo's property. The nursery receipts surface through financial investigation (Fumiko's contacts at local businesses, or records subpoenaed through the district police). Kenji finds the garden. The player pieces together the botanical timeline: one plant added after each disappearance. The two most recent nursery receipts — one approximately three weeks before Mira's death (Sora's plant), one the day after (Mira's sapling). The three-week gap maps the sequence. The garden proves pattern before the exchange proves mechanism.

**Reiko's static phone call:** Reiko contacts Kenji. Or Kenji contacts her — depending on player path. She describes something she never told anyone: a call from Mira, made from the school, with bad static and interference. Mira was urgent. Reiko was sleeping between shifts. "Call back later, the line is bad." Mira didn't call back. Reiko didn't know until later that this call was made from near the old cable runs — near the infrastructure that would later explain everything. The static wasn't random. It was the exchange bleeding through.

This call must land before the notebook scene in Ch 11. It establishes Reiko's final failure — the call she could have taken — so that the notebook scene has its full weight.

**Ogawa thread (deep, optional):** Three sub-layers available depending on investigation depth:
- *Standard:* Ogawa was cross-referencing Mira's observations independently. The firing was removal, not institutional randomness.
- *Deep (Nishida thread):* Before the cross-referencing, Ogawa filed a prior report about a teacher's aide named Nishida — ambiguous grooming concern. Endo's council buried it. Nishida was quietly transferred. The suppression worked: no scandal, no community fracture. This is the experiential foundation for Endo's philosophy — he was arguably right once, and that success became the template for burying Mira.
- *Almost-call:* Ogawa heard structured audio on a dead phone line in her classroom. The exchange was active, bleeding through the old cable runs. Foreshadows the infrastructure reveal in Ch 10. Mira was not the first signal to push through — she was the first one who got through.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** The framing is Endo's work. Haruki's words were weaponized. The garden proves the pattern. Sora was watching. The infrastructure pattern is emerging. Someone is alive and needs finding.

### Chapter 10 — Sora

**Player knows:** Sora may be alive. The garden proves pattern. The infrastructure map is nearly complete. Mira is weakening.

**Confirmation:** Kenji confirms through cross-referencing — Sora's disappearance timeline, Endo's access patterns, the nursery receipt gap — that Sora was taken, not run away, and may be held.

**Exchange room discovery:** Fumiko's infrastructure map, overlaid with Endo's knowledge pattern, reveals the signature. His information is consistently strongest near the old cable runs. The map doesn't prove murder. It proves access. The bridge number from Ch 3 — the anomaly the player logged as a question mark seven chapters ago — resolves here. It was an old exchange junction point. The structured static was the sound of infrastructure that never fully died. The player who revisits the bridge number after finding the exchange room hears the same patterned signal and understands what it was: the phone system remembering. Kenji enters the exchange room.

**Mira amplified:** Her voice clears. The static drops. She sounds *present.* She hears the other children — not words, impressions. The emotional residue of children who spoke into a system that absorbed them. "They're here. The other kids. They've been here the whole time." Beat. "Nobody picked up."

**Mira's understanding:** She grasps the mechanics of her own persistence. She is not haunting a phone — she is a signal in infrastructure built to carry signals like hers. She responds with silence, not deadpan. Then she sets the knowledge aside because there are other children in the walls.

**Mira hears Endo:** She doesn't gain Soul Read access. She gains proximity — his voice, his patterns, how he listens. "He's been listening to all of them. For years. He knows every voice in this town. He knows them better than they know themselves." Beat. "That's not care. That's collecting."

**Investigation pivots:** From solving a murder to preventing one. Mira is running out of time. The countdown begins.

**Player knowledge at chapter end:** Sora is alive. The exchange exists. Mira is amplified but fading. The final confrontation is coming.

---

## ACT IV: THE FINAL CALLS (Chapters 11–12)

### Chapter 11 — Cornering Endo

**Player knows:** The exchange, Endo's method, Sora's status. The case is circumstantial but dense.

**The notebook scene:** Kenji gives Reiko Mira's observation notebook. Not as evidence — as context. He needs her to understand what Mira actually was. Reiko reads the notebook. She discovers her daughter was tracking the community's failures with more sophistication than any adult in the investigation. She reads Mira's observations about herself — the entries about being dismissed, about "can't" and "won't" being different, about trying to explain things better. She reads what Mira saw and nobody believed.

This scene must be earned. It cannot happen until the player has spent hours with Mira. The player needs to know who Mira was before Reiko discovers it.

**Building the case:** Each NPC the player cultivated provides a piece. The nursery receipts. The planting dates. The infrastructure access records. Fumiko's documentation. The school reports Endo reviewed and dismissed. Kaito's social pattern logs. The framing evidence, each item traceable to Endo's access.

**Confronting Endo:** Requires everything the player has learned about dialogue mechanics. Endo does not break. He adjusts. "Let me help you" becomes "let me explain." The player presents contradictions — not to crack him, but to force redirections. The shape of his redirections maps the boundary of his secret.

---

### Chapter 12 — The Last Call

**Player knows:** Everything. The case. The exchange. The garden. The man.

**The switchboard duel — three interlocking systems running simultaneously:**

**System 1 — Call War (Social):** Endo realizes he's exposed. He begins calling everyone — Reiko, Haruki, Doi, the district police. Each call poisons a relationship the player spent the game building. The player counters with their own calls — reinforcing trust, presenting evidence. Limited call slots. Time pressure. Fifteen years of social capital vs. twelve chapters of investigation.

**System 2 — Infrastructure Race (Surveillance vs. Signal):** Mira, amplified by the exchange, intercepts Endo's calls. One at a time. The player chooses which to monitor. Every intercepted call reveals strategy. Every missed call costs ground. The exchange is degrading — Mira's amplified presence is overloading infrastructure never designed for a signal this strong.

**System 3 — Evidence Chain (Phoenix Wright Layer):** The player presents contradictions during calls with Endo. Each redirection reveals more of what he's protecting.

**Convergence — Sora:** Mira catches a fragment on the lines. A child. Afraid. Alive. Present tense. Sora is near an old cable run. His fear generates the same clarity-signal Mira generated.

**Mira's choice:** She can reach Sora. Phone to phone, through the switchboard Endo built. It will burn through whatever tether keeps her on the line. She tells Kenji. She does not ask permission.

"Someone is coming. His name is Kenji. He's grumpy but he listens. I need you to be brave for about twenty more minutes. Can you do that?"

"My name is Mira. Please remember it."

The line goes dead.

**Endo's arrest:** No final speech. No dramatic exit. Handcuffs and a car door closing. The framing lingers on the garden.

---

## ACT V: AFTER (Epilogue)

Six months later. Resolution scenes — each earned by the player's choices throughout:

- **Sora:** Recovered. Drawing cities again — different now, more open spaces. His map of real Yanagi with the X over the community center. The school essay: "She was the one who called."
- **Yui:** Safe with her grandmother. If the player intervened early, she is further along. If late, she is recovering but the delay is visible.
- **Reiko:** Read the notebook. Carries it. The relationship between mother and dead daughter has been recontextualized — not repaired, but understood.
- **Doi:** Custody situation revisited. The system that failed Mira is being examined. Doi's case is one of the things being reexamined.
- **Haruki:** Still teaching. Changed. Acting correctly inside a system that punishes correct action. The label he created has been struck from the record. The damage it did has not.
- **Fumiko:** Published the story. The full story, this time. Someone listened.
- **The Nishida Question:** The trial opened the files. The community learned about the buried report — the aide Ogawa flagged, the suppression Endo chose. Fumiko named the mechanism, not the man. The community was not careful. A name adjacent to an investigation. A question that couldn't be answered. Fujisawa did not repeat — but it rhymed. The truth saved a boy and arrested a monster. It also unsealed a question that had been sealed for a reason. Endo would have predicted this. He would have been right about the mechanism. He would still have been wrong about everything else.
- **Aizawa:** Still at the school. The procedures have been reviewed. She was not fired. She was not thanked.
- **Kaito:** The notebooks that made him a suspect became evidence that helped convict. He has not been thanked either. He has stopped carrying the notebooks. He does not explain why.
- **Rina:** The community that validated her social filtering is the same community now reckoning with what that filtering cost. Rina is eleven. She is processing this the way children process institutional shame — incompletely, without the framework to understand her role, aware that something she did mattered in a way nobody will explain to her directly.
- **Kenji:** New phone. Old one in the drawer with five others. Checks it sometimes. It doesn't ring.

The game returns to the title screen. The phone rings once. Nobody picks up.

## MIRA'S CRYING SCENE — PLACEMENT

The crying scene is the game's emotional center. It is deferrable but not skippable.

| Trigger | Chapter | Catalyst |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Primary | Ch 4 | Yui intervention succeeds — first time an adult acted on Mira's word |
| Deferred | Ch 5–6 | If Yui not yet acted on: the accumulation of dismissed reports in Haruki's school records triggers the same weight. Mira hears, for the first time, the full scope — three other children, same reports, same dismissals, same system. The realization that it wasn't just her, that it happened before and will happen again, produces the break through a different door. |

The scene itself is identical in emotional content regardless of trigger. What changes is the catalyst: in the primary version, Mira breaks because the system finally worked and the simplicity of that is unbearable. In the deferred version, she breaks because the system failed this many times and the scale of that is unbearable. Both arrive at the same truth — "it was never me" — from opposite directions.

If the deferred trigger fires, the player has spent an additional 1–2 chapters with armored Mira. The armor has been under more pressure. The break, when it comes, carries the weight of the delay — Mira has been holding longer, and the player knows it.

## YUI POST-RESCUE — DESIGN NOTE

After the intervention (whenever it fires), Yui is safe. She does not become an ongoing investigation source. This is deliberate: using a rescued abuse victim as an information tool would instrumentalize her in exactly the way the story critiques. Yui's investigative function is complete the moment her rescue triggers the crying scene. She validates Mira's credibility retroactively (Mira was right about Yui, therefore Mira was right about everything), but she does not provide case-specific evidence about Endo.

She is absent from the mid-late game chapters. She reappears in the epilogue. The player carries the knowledge that she is safe — and that knowledge, quiet and offscreen, is one of the few unambiguously good outcomes in the story.

## FUMIKO'S BLIND SPOT — PLACEMENT

## CALL SLOT ECONOMY

The phone is a finite resource. The player cannot call everyone every chapter. Who they call — and who they don't — shapes the investigation.

### Escalation by Act

**Act I (Ch 1–3) — Learning the phone.**
Generous. 3 available NPCs (Reiko, Doi, bridge). No competing demands. The player calls everyone, learns the loop (investigate → dial → read → reflect). The scarcity is numbers, not slots — the player wants MORE contacts, not fewer calls. This teaches the currency: phone numbers are the thing to hunt.

**Act II (Ch 4–7) — More NPCs than time.**
The roster expands faster than the player can service. By Ch 7, the player has access to Reiko, Doi, Rina, Yui, Kaito, Haruki, Aizawa, Fumiko — eight NPCs. Call sections allow 3–4 calls. The player must prioritize: who has information I need RIGHT NOW vs. who can I follow up later?

First trade-offs:
- Calling Rina provides social context but no evidence. Calling Kaito provides evidence but is slow and uncomfortable.
- Calling Fumiko opens a powerful source but starts a relationship with its own timeline (the publication risk).
- Calling Haruki repeatedly keeps him coordinated but costs slots that could go to new leads.

**Act III (Ch 8–10) — Every call has a cost.**
Endo is on the board. Calling him produces real leads that point away from him. NOT calling him means missing actionable information the investigation needs. The trap: Endo is the highest-value NPC per call slot, and he's the antagonist.

Additional pressure: Mira's degradation means post-call reads take longer and provide less. The player's most reliable analytical tool is fading. Calls must be chosen not just for information value but for read value — which NPC will produce the read that matters most while Mira can still deliver it?

**Act IV (Ch 11–12) — The phone is a battlefield.**
Ch 11: The player's call slots build the case. Each NPC provides one piece. The player chooses which pieces to assemble and in what order.

Ch 12: Real-time call war. Endo is burning through calls to every NPC simultaneously. The player has limited counter-calls. Every slot spent reinforcing one relationship is a slot not spent on another. Endo has fifteen years of social capital. The player has twelve chapters of trust. The economy inverts: instead of "who should I call?", the question becomes "who can I afford to lose?"

### Off-Screen NPC Communication

NPCs talk to each other between the player's calls. This is not random — it follows social logic:

| If the player calls... | These NPCs may learn about it... | Because... |
|------------------------|----------------------------------|------------|
| Reiko | Haruki, school contacts | Reiko tells people the detective called |
| Rina | Community gossip network | Rina processes social information and redistributes it |
| Haruki (with case details) | The family he visits (PoNR) | He acts on information the player shared |
| Fumiko (with too much) | Fumiko's publication timeline accelerates | She has a story and a deadline only she controls |
| Endo | Endo adjusts off-screen | He recalibrates after every call — which leads he pushes, which NPCs he contacts |
| Doi (during community pressure) | Community suspicion increases | The investigation's contact with the "suspect" is visible |

**The Fumiko Publication Timer:**
Fumiko is the most dangerous ally because she has her own timeline. If the player keeps her informed — shares enough that she feels like a partner — she holds. She waits. If the player shuts her out, she publishes what she has: incomplete, circumstantial, enough to alert Endo that the investigation is circling. The player manages this not through a visible meter but through call content — what they share, what they withhold, whether they treat her as a partner or a source.

## MIRA'S DEGRADATION CURVE

Mira's signal decays across the game. The player feels this mechanically (reads degrade) and emotionally (her voice changes).

| Chapter | Signal State | Manifestation |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| 1–3 | **Strong** | Comments freely between calls. Unsolicited observations about evidence, about Kenji, about the neighborhood. Full personality — sardonic, precise, testing. The player takes her constant presence for granted. |
| 4–5 | **First waver** | Occasional pauses where there shouldn't be pauses. Asks Kenji to repeat something once. A word arrives slightly after the meaning, like a subtitle lagging behind dialogue. Easy to miss. Easy to attribute to the phone connection. |
| 6–7 | **Noticeable decline** | Goes quiet during transitions between investigation and calls. Loses words — reaches for a description and settles for something less precise. Meaning arrives after sound. She stops making unsolicited jokes. The absence of humor is what the player notices. |
| 8–9 | **Degraded** | Speaks mainly during reads. When she talks freely, it's shorter, stripped of sarcasm. She is conserving. Reads take noticeably longer — the same Soul Read that was instant in Ch 3 now has a delay. The player adjusts their call strategy around what Mira can still deliver. |
| 10 | **Temporarily amplified** | The exchange room clears her signal. For one chapter, she sounds like early-game Mira — present, clear, close. The contrast with Ch 8–9 is devastating. The player remembers what they've been losing. The amplification is the exchange's infrastructure carrying her signal the way it was designed to carry voices. It won't last. |
| 11 | **Post-amplification crash** | The amplification cost. Worse than before — reads cut out mid-delivery, her voice drops to near-static between calls. The exchange is degrading and so is she. The player feels the countdown in the silence between her words. |
| 12 | **Final signal** | One last clear transmission: the call to Sora. Everything she has left goes into twenty words to a frightened boy. Then static. Then the line goes dead. The signal that was the strongest thing the exchange ever carried becomes part of the infrastructure — another impression in the copper, another voice. |

**Design principle:** The degradation is never announced. No UI element tracks Mira's signal. The player notices because they've been listening to her voice for twelve chapters and they know what she sounds like. The game teaches the player to listen — and then it takes away the thing they learned to listen to.

## DIALOGUE MECHANIC PROGRESSION

The five intents are learned through NPC encounters. Each NPC teaches a lesson about which approach works — and the lessons compound.

### Teaching Sequence

| Intent | Primary teacher | Chapter | Lesson |
|--------|----------------|---------|--------|
| **Pressure** | Doi (first call) | Ch 3 | Pressure produces defensiveness, not information. The gruff man gets gruffer. First lesson: pushing harder is not always productive. |
| **Reassure** | Yui | Ch 4 | Trust-building opens guarded NPCs. But Reassure is too slow for Yui specifically — she doesn't need comfort, she needs safety. Reassure works on SOME people. Not all. |
| **Silence** | Yui + Doi | Ch 4, Ch 6 | The game's dominant mechanic. Silence lets Yui fill space with fragments. Silence lets Doi's confession collapse. Silence is what every adult in Mira's life failed to provide — the space to be heard. The player who learns Silence has learned the game's thesis. |
| **Redirect** | Aizawa | Ch 5 | Procedural walls can't be pushed through or waited out. They must be navigated around. Redirect shifts the conversation's axis — the player asks about X, Aizawa answers about Y, and Y is more revealing than X would have been. |
| **Bluff** | Fumiko | Ch 6–7 | Claiming knowledge you don't have. High risk: Fumiko sees through bad bluffs instantly ("Don't insult me"). High reward: a successful bluff makes Fumiko believe the player has more evidence than they do, which accelerates her information sharing. |

### Compounding Through the Game

**Act I:** Player uses all five intents experimentally. No clear best strategy. The game rewards variety — different NPCs respond to different approaches.

**Act II:** Silence emerges as the most rewarded mechanic. Two of the game's biggest emotional payoffs (Yui's fragments, Doi's collapse) come from the player choosing not to speak. The game is training the player that listening is a tool — the same lesson the story is about.

**Act III — The Endo Problem:** Endo breaks the pattern. Silence doesn't work — he fills it comfortably, with helpful information that points away from him. Reassure doesn't work — he doesn't need trust, he offers it. Pressure doesn't work — he doesn't crack, he adjusts. Bluff is dangerous — if he catches it, he recalibrates.

**Redirect is the key to Endo.** The player who has been trained on Silence must now learn a new dominant strategy: not listening for what Endo says, but steering him into revealing what he avoids. Each redirect forces Endo to choose which direction to go. The shape of his avoidances maps the boundary of his secret. The player who tracks WHERE Endo redirects can triangulate what he's protecting.

**Act IV:** All five intents deployed simultaneously during the switchboard duel. Different NPCs in the call war require different approaches: Reassure for Reiko (hold her trust), Pressure for the district police (force urgency), Redirect for Endo (map his boundary), Silence when intercepting (let him reveal strategy), Bluff when the evidence chain has gaps. The player who has mastered the full toolkit uses everything. The player who relied on one strategy is outmatched.

## NPC-TO-NPC RELATIONSHIP WEB

NPCs exist in a social network that responds to the player's actions. This web determines the consequences of choices.

### Key Relationships

**Haruki ↔ Fumiko:** They don't know each other before the investigation. They meet through Kenji. Immediately productive, immediately tense. Haruki has institutional access. Fumiko has historical context. The player needs both. In Ch 7–8, they pull the investigation in opposite directions: Haruki wants to push a lead immediately; Fumiko wants to hold and build the full pattern. The player must choose whose instinct to follow.

**Endo → Everyone:** Endo's social capital connects to every NPC. He is Reiko's community support. He chairs Haruki's school committee. He is Doi's neighborhood councilman. He is Aizawa's institutional superior (indirectly, through the safety council). He knows Fumiko is digging and has positioned himself as indispensable to her professional life. Every call the player makes to an NPC passes through Endo's social field. In Ch 12, he activates these connections as weapons.

**Reiko ↔ The Community:** Reiko's position in Yanagi means the investigation's contact with her is visible. Endo uses this in Ch 12 — framing the investigation as re-traumatizing a grieving mother.

**Kaito — Isolated:** Kaito has no social connections to other NPCs. This is both his vulnerability (no one to vouch for him) and his protection (no one Endo can use to reach him). His evidence exists outside the social web.

### Branching Consequences Beyond Haruki's PoNR

| Decision | Chapter | Consequence |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Yui timing** | Ch 4 vs. deferred | Crying scene trigger, Mira's register shift timing, Yui's epilogue state |
| **Accepting Doi's confession** | Ch 6 | Wrong path — investigation stalls on a false lead, time lost, Doi is damaged further |
| **Following Fumiko's blind spot** | Ch 7 | Time lost during convergence, but the lesson (verify Fumiko) pays off in the late game |
| **Information shared with Fumiko** | Ch 6–9 | Too much → premature publication, Endo alerted, social web destabilized. Too little → Fumiko publishes independently with incomplete information, possibly harming an innocent. |
| **Endo call strategy** | Ch 8–11 | Every call to Endo produces real leads + adjustments. More calls = more information but more recalibration time for Endo. Fewer calls = Endo operates blind but the player has less evidence. |
| **Ch 12 call war allocation** | Ch 12 | Each NPC the player fails to reinforce may be turned by Endo. NPC loyalty in the endgame is earned across the whole game, not decided in the final chapter. |

### The True Ending Condition

The true ending requires: Yui safe (at any point) + Sora rescued + Endo arrested with sufficient evidence. The evidence threshold depends on how many NPCs contributed pieces and how many relationships survived Endo's Ch 12 call war. A player who cultivated every relationship has redundancy — losing one NPC to Endo doesn't collapse the case. A player who focused narrowly has a fragile evidence chain that Endo can break with a single well-placed call.

The game does not announce this. The player discovers it through outcome — the ending they get reflects the web they built.
