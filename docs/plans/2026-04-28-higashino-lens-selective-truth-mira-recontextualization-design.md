## Point 6: Recontextualizing Mira Through the Yui Sequence

### The Principle

Mira's school reputation — "the girl who says things," "disruptive honesty," "intense" — was not built by scattered reports about many things. It was built by one thing: Yui is being hurt and nobody will help. Every report that followed, about the silver car, about Endo, about patterns near the school, was filtered through a credibility deficit that the Yui reports created. Mira didn't understand this connection. She just kept telling the truth. The system did the rest.

This is the Higashino move: not a hidden act of heroism. A visible, persistent, documented truth that everyone saw and nobody understood because they'd already decided what kind of person was speaking it.

### Investigation Layer (Ch 5-9): The Institutional Sequence

The player discovers the dates. Through school records (Aizawa's files, Haruki's testimony, the behavioral classification file from the companion Higashino-lens design), the player can reconstruct the chronology:

- Mira's first report to a teacher (about Yui) predates every other report in her file
- The "disruptive" classification originates from the fallout of the Yui report — the teacher called Yui's mother, the mother told the boyfriend, things got worse, and Mira was flagged for "causing disruption to a family situation"
- Every subsequent report — the silver car, the man near the school, the behavioral patterns — entered the system already tagged by the first classification. The dismissals weren't independent decisions. They were downstream of the original label.

The player who tracks the dates across Haruki's testimony (Ch 5), the school behavioral file (Ch 5-7, already in the companion design as Haruki's "weaponized language" evidence), and Aizawa's records (Ch 7-9) can see the sequence. The notebook auto-logs key dates when they surface, so the timeline is visible. But the evidence is institutional — filing language, classification codes, dates. It tells the player what the system recorded, not what Mira experienced.

### Notebook Layer (Ch 11): Mira's Voice

The notebook scene already exists. The enhancement is the first entry.

The first entry in Mira's observation notebook — the one Reiko reads first, the one that starts the fourteen-month log ��� is about Yui. Not by name (Mira protects her, even in the notebook). But unmistakable. Something in the register of:

> "I told Mr. Ise about [her]. He called her mom. Her mom called [him]. Now it's worse. I made it worse. I don't know what to do. I'm going to try again but different this time."

Then the entries the player already sees — the silver car, Endo's proximity, the escalating reports — read differently. They're not a child who reports everything. They're a child who reported one thing, was punished for it, and kept going anyway, broadening her scope because the direct approach failed and she was trying to find the angle that would finally make an adult act.

The last entry — already in the manuscript — becomes retroactively devastating: "I think I know what's happening. I'm going to tell someone tomorrow. Not mom. Not Ms. Aizawa." Fourteen months later. Different strategy, same engine.

### What Changes in the Player's Understanding

**Before the recontextualization:** Mira was a perceptive child who reported many things and was dismissed because the system couldn't handle her intensity.

**After:** The system didn't dismiss her because she was intense. It dismissed her because her first act of truth-telling — trying to protect Yui — was classified as disruption, and that classification became the lens through which every subsequent truth was processed. Her intensity didn't create the dismissal. Her compassion did. The system used her love for Yui to build the wall that her reports about Endo couldn't get past.

### What Mira Didn't Understand

Mira knew she was being dismissed. She knew Yui needed help. She didn't connect the two — didn't see that the Yui reports were why the Endo reports were dismissed. She thought the problem was her delivery, her tone, herself. ("I thought there was something wrong with me.") The crying scene in Ch 4 gains a new layer: she wasn't wrong about the system. But the specific mechanism — her own compassion weaponized into her classification — is something only the player and Kenji can see, because it requires the institutional sequence a nine-year-old couldn't access.

### Relationship to Companion Design

The Yui-report sequence intersects with several through-lines from the companion Higashino-lens design:

- **Haruki's weaponized language:** The phrase he wrote in Mira's file ("disruptive honesty" or its equivalent) originated from the Yui report fallout. His hidden act of selfishness gains additional weight — he didn't just label Mira's truth-telling. He labeled the specific truth-telling that was trying to protect another child.
- **Aizawa's administrative burial:** Her classification of Mira's notebook as "personal effects, non-evidentiary" gains a new dimension — the notebook that starts with the Yui entry was the one she chose not to read.
- **Rina's social commission:** The notebook incident from second grade maps to the same period as the Yui reports. Mira's social isolation and her advocacy for Yui were happening simultaneously, reinforcing each other.

## Point 3: Endo's Selective Truth as Narrative Principle

### The Principle

Endo never lies. This is not a limitation — it's his method. He selects which true things reach which people, and the curation itself is invisible because every individual piece checks out. The switchboard is the literal version of what he does socially: route signal. Truth A goes to person A. Truth B goes to person B. Truth A and Truth B, if they were ever in the same room, would produce understanding. They never are.

### What Already Exists

The manuscript has Endo doing this across multiple scenes:

- **Ch 8:** Three helpful leads, each real, each pointed away from him. Preemptive framing of Fumiko as unreliable.
- **Ch 11:** The confrontation, where Endo redirects rather than denies.
- **Ch 12:** The call war — different register for each person, different true framing for each audience.

### Sharpening Moves

No new mechanic. Three narrative adjustments:

**1. The Partition Pattern Across Ch 8 Tells**

Endo's three leads in Ch 8 each contain a true thing that points away from him. The sharpening: each lead also contains a true thing that would point toward him if the player heard it alongside information Endo gave someone else. The Ch 12 intercepts retroactively reveal what Endo told other people, and the player who compares notes realizes: same event, different audiences, different truths delivered, one truth withheld.

Example: the Ogawa staffing change. Endo tells Kenji about it as context. He told Haruki about it as administrative. He didn't mention it to Reiko — because Reiko might connect it to Mira's reports. The notebook auto-logs each version when it surfaces. The attentive player sees the entries accumulating and notices they are the same event described differently depending on who's hearing it.

**2. Mira Names It (Ch 10)**

During the amplification scene, Mira already says "That's not care. That's collecting." The sharpening adds a second observation — not about his listening, but about his routing. Something in the register of:

> "He doesn't hide things. He sorts them. Everyone gets a piece. Nobody gets the picture."

One line. Mira's voice. Gives the player the vocabulary to describe what they've been sensing. Mirrors the switchboard literally: an operator doesn't block calls. He routes them.

**3. Ch 12 Intercepts as Reveal**

The intercepts already exist. The sharpening: each intercept contains one detail that connects to something Endo told Kenji in Ch 8, reframed for the new audience. The player who remembers Ch 8 hears the echo and realizes: Ch 12 isn't new behavior. It's the same behavior at emergency speed.

The player's realization: the call war isn't Endo panicking. It's Endo doing what he always does. The only reason the player can see it now is because Mira is in the switchboard, hearing all the calls simultaneously. The exchange gives the player (through Mira) what no one in Yanagi ever had: all of Endo's truths in one room.

### What This Changes About Endo

Endo's design thesis ("truth must be contained") gains mechanical specificity. He doesn't contain truth by suppressing it. He contains it by distributing it — giving everyone enough to feel informed, never enough to act, and ensuring the pieces never converge. The community didn't lack information. It lacked adjacency.

## Point 5: Sora — No Changes

The existing design is the design. Mira and Sora never spoke while alive. The final call derives its power from their being strangers connected by structural kinship. "You fit" lands because it comes from a boy who never knew her. Dramatizing a missed connection would turn them into almost-friends and convert the final call from recognition into reunion. Leave it.

## Convergence: How Points 3 and 6 Dovetail

Point 6 reveals that Mira's credibility deficit was built by the system processing her Yui reports �� institutional habit converting compassion into classification.

Point 3 reveals that Endo's power comes from ensuring the system never assembles the full picture — deliberate curation keeping truths partitioned across the community.

The player arrives at the final confrontation understanding both halves: the system failed Mira not because it lacked information, but because the information was partitioned — by institutional habit (the classification that followed Mira from the Yui reports) and by deliberate curation (Endo's routing). One was accidental. One was intentional. Both produced the same result: a child's truth, distributed across a community, never assembled into action.

This is the game's thesis stated through mechanism rather than dialogue. The system that killed Mira was not one monster. It was a community where truth was always present and never adjacent.

## Design Principle: The Higashino Recontextualization

The strongest recontextualization does not add new information. It rearranges existing information so the player re-experiences everything they already knew.

The player who reaches the notebook's first entry has already seen:
- Mira's school file with its "disruptive" classification
- The dates showing the Yui reports came first
- Haruki's language in the behavioral file
- Aizawa's procedural processing of every subsequent report
- Twelve chapters of Mira's armor, her testing, her "I thought there was something wrong with me"

The first entry doesn't tell them something new. It makes them hear everything they already have in a different key. That is the Higashino move: the truth was always visible. The reveal is learning to read it.
