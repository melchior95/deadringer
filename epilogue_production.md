# EPILOGUE — After (Production Specification)

## Six Months Later

---

## STRUCTURAL OVERVIEW

**Total runtime estimate:** ~9–11 minutes (resolution scenes) + ~30 seconds (title screen return)

**Sequence:** The eleven resolution scenes play in fixed order — Sora, Yui, Reiko, Doi, Haruki, Fumiko, The Nishida Question, Aizawa, Kaito, Rina, Kenji — followed by the title screen return. The order is not negotiable: it begins with the rescued child (the unambiguous good), ends with the detective alone (the bittersweet residue), and routes through ascending complexity (private grief → public reckoning → community-scale moral ambiguity → institutional residue → the man who keeps dead phones).

**Player input:** Minimal. The epilogue is largely cutscene. Two scenes (Reiko, Kenji) allow brief environmental examination. No dialogue choices. No notebook entries. The notebook system is closed; the player is no longer investigating.

**Master audio principle:** Silence is the dominant texture. Score is rare and must be earned. Where a scene's power comes from quiet, the spec calls for quiet — never under-scored, never over-scored. No emotional swells. No telling the player how to feel. The game has spent twelve chapters teaching the player to listen. The epilogue is the listening.

**Master visual principle:** Locked or near-locked compositions. Slow camera, infrequent cuts. No handheld. No dramatic angles. The epilogue's visual register is "after the storm" — the world resumed, but resumed differently.

**Branching architecture:** Three scenes carry meaningful branches from earlier-chapter decisions (Yui, Doi, Kenji). One scene carries a soft branch (Sora, depending on artifact tracking). The remaining scenes play identically regardless of player choices — the resolution is the same; the player's path to it is what differed.

### SORA

**Location:** Hayashi Sora's classroom — Nishi-Yanagi Municipal Elementary, Grade 3, Room 3-B (new school, post-rescue placement). Afternoon, weekday.
**Runtime:** ~75 seconds
**Format:** Mixed — primarily cutscene with one optional examine point (the essay close-up)
**Transition in:** From the post-credits black, a slow fade-up to the classroom. The first scene of the epilogue. The black holds approximately three seconds longer than feels comfortable before the fade begins — letting the player register that the game is continuing.
**Score:** None — ambient only. Distant playground sounds, the building's HVAC, a faint cicada outside (the epilogue is set in the warm season; six months from a winter case). No music cue.
**Branching:** Soft. If the player logged Sora's bench artifact in Ch 2 and the graph paper fragment, the in-classroom map is presented in close-up first (the player recognizes the medium). If the player did not log those artifacts, the map enters the frame as part of a wider establishing shot — still legible, but uncontextualized. Either path arrives at the same essay.

[VISUAL: A classroom. Afternoon light through windows that need cleaning. Third grade, reassembled — the desks arranged in clusters, the walls covered in student work. Among the drawings and essays and science projects: a map.]

[AUDIO: Ambient classroom-after-hours. Distant voices from the corridor. The faint mechanical hum of an old wall clock. A fan somewhere. No children in this room — recess or end-of-day. The room has the specific quiet of a space that was loud an hour ago and will be loud again tomorrow.]

[CAMERA: Wide establishing shot of the classroom held for four seconds. Then a slow push-in toward the wall of student work. The map enters frame center as the camera arrives. Hold.]

[Not the imaginary cities. A real one. Yanagi — rendered in colored pencil on graph paper, the same medium, the same structural precision, but different. The buildings are there. The streets connect. The transit lines run. But the spaces between them are wider. More parks. More open areas. More room for people to sit and exist in each other's presence without needing to talk about it.]

[In the center of the map, where the community center stands, Sora has drawn an X. Red. Small. Precise. The X of a child who knows what was under the building and has decided to mark it the way cartographers mark things that are dangerous and known.]

[CAMERA: Cut to the teacher's desk. Medium shot, slightly downward angle — the player's POV is approximately the height of a standing adult.]

[On the teacher's desk: a school essay. The assignment was "Someone Important." Most students wrote about parents or grandparents or athletes or characters from television.]

[Sora's essay is three paragraphs. The handwriting is careful, still mixing up the stroke order on two kanji — the same two Mira mixed up, though neither of them knew that about the other.]

[DESIGN NOTE — KANJI DETAIL: This is a quiet symmetry the player is not asked to recognize. The two kanji should be selected from common third-grade vocabulary where stroke-order errors are plausible. If the game is localized, the visual error must be preserved — even readers who can't parse the kanji should see that two characters have a particular wobble that matches Mira's notebook (referenced in Ch 11's notebook scene).]

[CAMERA: Push-in to essay close-up. The handwriting fills the frame. The player can read at their own pace. There is no advance prompt — the scene holds until the player taps to continue.]

*The most important person I know is someone I never met. Her name is Mira. She called me on a phone that doesn't work, in a room where phones don't work, and told me someone was coming.*

*She said his name was Kenji and that he listens. She was right. He came and he listened and I went home.*

*She was the one who called.*

[AUDIO: During the essay reading, the ambient drops a touch. Not silence. The corridor voices fade. The fan stays. The clock stays. The room is letting the writing be heard.]

[The essay has a grade. The teacher wrote a note in red: "Beautiful writing, Sora. Would you like to tell us more about Mira?" He has not responded to the note. Some things are complete as written.]

[CAMERA: Pull back from the essay. Hold on the desk for two seconds. The map remains visible in the background, slightly out of focus.]

[DESIGN NOTE — VOICE: No voice-over for the essay. The text is read silently by the player. Mira's voice does not return. If a future production decision adds VO, it must be Sora's voice, not Mira's — and even Sora's voice should be considered carefully against the principle that Mira does not come back. Default: silent text.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Three-second hold on black before next scene fades up. The transition between epilogue scenes is consistent: fade-out, sustained black, fade-up. Each scene is given its own room.

### YUI

**Location:** Sakamoto Hideko's residence (Yui's grandmother) — exterior yard, suburban district outside Yanagi. Late afternoon.
**Runtime:** ~50 seconds
**Format:** Cutscene. No interactive elements. Yui does not speak. The scene is observation.
**Transition in:** Fade-up from black after the Sora close. The fade is slightly faster than Sora's — Yui's scene is shorter and quieter, and the rhythm benefits from arriving without preamble.
**Score:** None — ambient only. Outdoor afternoon sound. Wind through the garden. A wind chime somewhere (small, deliberate, not melodic). A neighbor's distant television. The sound of a place where nothing is happening, which is the point.
**Branching:** YES — primary branch. Player's Ch 4 timing on the Yui intervention determines Yui's recovery state. Two visual variants:
  - **Early intervention path (Ch 4 acted-on):** Yui's posture is open. She is seated in the yard, not against a wall. The book is held at reading distance, two-handed. She turns a page during the held shot. The grandmother is visible in the background through a kitchen window, not watching Yui — doing her own work.
  - **Deferred intervention path (Ch 5–6 acted-on):** Yui's posture is partially closed. She is seated near the house wall, the book held closer to her body. She does not turn a page during the held shot. The grandmother is visible in the background through the kitchen window, periodically looking out at Yui, not interrupting.
  Both variants are visually similar. The differences are subtle and read on a second viewing. The audio is identical between branches.

[VISUAL: A different neighborhood. A house with a small yard. Yui's grandmother's home — where Yui has lived since Chapter 4's intervention. The yard has a garden, smaller and less organized than Endo's, planted by a woman who gardens because she likes watching things grow, not because each plant means something terrible.]

[AUDIO: Outdoor ambient. The wind through leaves. A wind chime, single tone, irregular. Distant — a child laughing somewhere down the street, briefly. A door closing somewhere indoors.]

[CAMERA: Wide establishing shot of the yard from the street side. The fence is low. The viewer is invited in by the framing, not blocked out. Hold for three seconds, then a slow push-in to medium.]

[Yui is in the yard. She is reading. Not hiding — reading. The difference is in the posture: a child who reads to disappear holds the book like a shield. A child who reads because she wants to holds it like a window. Yui holds it like a window.]

[DESIGN NOTE: The player's timing in Ch 4 affects this scene. If the player intervened early, Yui is further along — the grandmother's house feels like home, not refuge. If the player waited, Yui is recovering but the delay is visible: she still flinches at sudden sounds, still checks the door when she hears footsteps. The grandmother is patient either way. Recovery is not a straight line.]

[CAMERA: Hold on Yui in medium shot for approximately twenty seconds. The composition does not change. The viewer sits with the image. In the deferred path, this hold is harder — the player has earned a less complete resolution and the camera does not look away from that.]

[Yui does not appear in the epilogue beyond this image. She does not need to speak. She is reading in a garden in the afternoon and nobody is hurting her. The game has earned this by spending four chapters working toward it. The player who remembers the Yui from Chapter 4 — the girl who filled silence with carefully measured fragments — sees a different silence here. Comfortable. Chosen.]

[AUDIO: The wind chime sounds once during the hold. Then a long quiet.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Hold on black four seconds before next scene — Yui's scene is given the most darkness around it of any epilogue beat. The silence she has been given is the silence we hold around her.

### REIKO

**Location:** Kitahara Reiko's apartment — kitchen and adjoining living room, low table visible. Mid-morning. Same apartment from Ch 3, Ch 9, Ch 11.
**Runtime:** ~70 seconds
**Format:** Mixed — cutscene with one optional examine point (the photograph on the refrigerator). Reiko does not appear on camera. The apartment is the storyteller.
**Transition in:** Fade-up from extended black. The interior light should match Ch 11's notebook scene — the same morning quality, the same window angle. This is deliberate continuity. Six months have passed and the apartment looks the same, except for what has changed.
**Score:** None — ambient only. The refrigerator hum. A kettle, off-camera, finishing its boil and clicking off mid-scene. Distant traffic from outside the window. A neighbor's footsteps in the corridor.
**Branching:** None. Reiko's resolution does not branch. The notebook scene in Ch 11 is the gate; if the player reached the ending, they reached the notebook scene; this resolution follows.

[VISUAL: Reiko's apartment. The same apartment — same kitchen, same living room, same hallway closet where Mira's school uniforms hang, pressed, in order. But the low table has something on it that wasn't there before.]

[AUDIO: Refrigerator hum. The specific quiet of a small apartment in the late morning when the occupant is somewhere in the next room.]

[CAMERA: Wide shot of the living room from the kitchen-side angle. The low table is in mid-frame. Hold three seconds. Then push-in toward the table.]

[The notebook. Mira's observation log. It sits on the table the way a book sits on the table of someone who reads it regularly — spine cracked, a bookmark partway through, the cover showing the wear of hands that open it often.]

[CAMERA: Close-up of the notebook on the table. The bookmark is a folded receipt — a domestic object, not ceremonial. The notebook is being lived with, not enshrined. Hold five seconds.]

[CAMERA: Cut to the kitchen. Medium. The refrigerator is in frame.]

[Reiko is in the kitchen. The meal prep containers are in the refrigerator. The star is on the nikujaga lid. But the container is not full. Someone has been eating it.]

[AUDIO: The kettle clicks off. The sound is small but present — the only mechanical event in the scene. It marks the passage of time without scoring it.]

[She does not appear in the scene. The apartment tells the story: a woman who carried her daughter's observations for fourteen months without reading them now reads them every week. The nikujaga, made from her mother's recipe with the specific brand of soy sauce from the larger supermarket two stops away — she still makes it. She has started eating it.]

[DESIGN NOTE: Reiko's absence from her own scene is deliberate. She has been the most photographed grief in the game — three call scenes, the static call, the notebook scene. The epilogue grants her privacy. The player has earned the right to see her apartment; they have not earned the right to look at her face here. The room is the resolution.]

[The relationship between a mother and her dead daughter has not been repaired. It has been recontextualized. Reiko understands now what Mira was, what Mira was doing, and what Reiko's failure cost. That understanding does not produce forgiveness — from either side. It produces something more durable: accuracy. Reiko now sees Mira the way Mira always saw the world. Clearly. Without the filter.]

[CAMERA: The shot drifts toward the refrigerator. The player can examine the photograph if desired — a single tap brings the image to full frame. Otherwise, the camera holds wide and the photograph is visible but not emphasized.]

[On the refrigerator, held by a magnet: a photograph of Mira. Not a memorial photograph — not the formal school picture that appeared in the news. A candid one. Mira at the kitchen table, notebook open, pencil in hand, looking up at the camera with the expression of a child interrupted mid-observation. Annoyed. Focused. Alive.]

[AUDIO: The photograph examine, if triggered, plays in dead silence. No audio cue. The image is its own statement.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Hold on black three seconds.

### DOI

**Location:** Yanagi Mart — corner store interior visible from street, exterior bench across the road in second beat. Late afternoon, golden hour beginning.
**Runtime:** ~60 seconds
**Format:** Cutscene. No interactive elements. Doi does not speak.
**Transition in:** Fade-up from black. The light is warmer than the previous scenes — the day has moved on. This is the first temporal advancement within the epilogue's "six months later" frame.
**Score:** None — ambient only. Street sounds. A bicycle passing. The store's interior bell when the door opens (off-screen — a customer entering or leaving, never seen).
**Branching:** YES — secondary branch. The presence of the bench scratch in the second beat depends on whether the player believed Doi in Ch 6 (rejected the false confession) AND whether the player examined the bench in Ch 2.
  - **Believed Doi + examined bench in Ch 2:** The bench scratch is shown. The player recognizes the medium and the hand.
  - **Believed Doi, did not examine bench in Ch 2:** The bench scratch is shown but its significance is muted — the player sees a mark on a bench, the connection to Sora is implicit but unsignaled.
  - **Accepted Doi's false confession in Ch 6:** The bench scratch is still present (Sora draws regardless of the player's beliefs about Doi), but the camera composition de-emphasizes it. The bench is in frame but not centered. The viewer who knows what to look for sees it; the viewer who doesn't passes over it. The scene plays slightly shorter (~50 seconds) and the second beat carries no narrative weight beyond "the bench is there." The custody-revisit text appears in all variants — Doi's case is being re-examined regardless of player belief.

[VISUAL: The corner shop. Late afternoon. Doi behind the counter, the same as always — gruff, watchful, the old man who has been part of the street for longer than most of the street has been standing.]

[AUDIO: Street ambient. A scooter passing. The faint sound of the store's radio, low — a baseball game, indistinct, the kind of background noise that doesn't ask to be listened to.]

[CAMERA: Exterior shot of the storefront from across the street. Wide. Doi is visible through the window. Hold five seconds. The camera does not push in. We are observing him from the same distance the neighborhood observes him.]

[The custody hearing has been revisited. The system that failed Mira — the community oversight structure that Endo chaired, the same structure that processed complaints, dismissed reports, and maintained the surface — is being examined. Doi's case is one of the things being reexamined. Not because Doi was innocent — the situation was always more complicated than innocence — but because the process that evaluated him was corrupted by the man who ran it.]

[Doi does not discuss this. He is not the kind of man who discusses things. He stands behind his counter and sells tea and rice and instant curry to the neighborhood that no longer quite trusts itself. The community that maintained the surface is learning what the surface cost, and the learning is slow and incomplete and does not produce the clean reckoning that stories promise.]

[CAMERA: Slow pan from the storefront across the street to the bench. The pan is approximately four seconds and tracks at the pace of someone walking. The bench enters frame from the right side and the camera holds when it is centered.]

[The bench across the street — Sora's bench, where a boy once sat and drew cities — has a new scratch in the wood. Small. Recent. A diagram: intersecting lines, transit routes, the infrastructure of an imaginary place that works the way the real one should have. Sora has been back.]

[CAMERA: Close-up on the bench scratch (in the believed-Doi paths). The marks are precise, child-made, identical in style to the Ch 2 bench artifact. Hold three seconds.]

[DESIGN NOTE — BRANCHING SUBTLETY: The believed-Doi reward is recognition, not exposition. The player who tracked Sora's artifacts since Ch 2 and trusted Doi when others did not now sees that the boy Doi noticed all those months ago is the boy who came back. Doi's testimony was real. Doi's gap-in-routine observation about the drawing boy was real. The bench is small evidence that the world the false confession would have erased — the world in which Doi was an observer, not a perpetrator — is the world the player helped restore. None of this is text. It is the camera looking at a scratch on a bench.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Hold on black three seconds.

### HARUKI

**Location:** Yanagi Municipal Elementary — Haruki's classroom (Ch 5 setting), pre-school morning. Empty room.
**Runtime:** ~65 seconds
**Format:** Cutscene. No interactive elements. Haruki does not speak. Voiceover or internal monologue is NOT used.
**Transition in:** Fade-up from black. Morning light, cool — the day has reset. This is the first scene of the epilogue's "second day" feel, even though the timeline is unspecified.
**Score:** None — ambient only. Empty-school sounds: a custodian's footsteps somewhere in the building, a distant door closing, the building's old plumbing settling. No children yet.
**Branching:** None. Haruki's resolution does not branch. The Ch 6 PoNR and Ch 9 break play out the same way regardless; the epilogue resolution is identical.

[VISUAL: The school. Morning. Haruki in his classroom, before the students arrive. He is organizing papers — the same restless energy, the same inability to be still, but directed differently now. The recklessness is gone. The cold precision from his break has softened into something sustainable: attention. Not the desperate attention of a man trying to redeem himself. The steady attention of a man who has learned what inattention costs.]

[AUDIO: The classroom's specific quiet before the day starts. A pen on a desk. The slight squeak of a chair. The fluorescent lights' high-frequency hum, almost inaudible. The distant clatter of someone in the staff room.]

[CAMERA: Medium-wide shot of the classroom from the doorway angle. Haruki is at his desk, three-quarters frame. Hold for the first paragraph of text.]

[The label he created — "disruptive honesty" — has been struck from Mira's file. Officially expunged during the review of the safety council's records. The phrase that appeared in the framing documents no longer appears in any official record.]

[The damage it did has not been struck. The phrase entered the community's vocabulary. Parents used it. Teachers repeated it. The label attached to Mira the way labels attach to children — silently, adhesively, reshaping how adults processed everything she said. Removing it from the record does not remove it from the people who used it to not listen.]

[CAMERA: Cut to a closer shot — medium, side angle, Haruki at his desk in profile. He is sorting papers with the efficient precision of a man who has been doing this same task for years and knows exactly how long it takes. The sorting is not performative. It is work.]

[Haruki teaches. He has not left. He acts correctly inside a system that punishes correct action — filing reports through proper channels, following up on student concerns, calling parents when the situation warrants it. He does this knowing that the system will process his diligence the way it always has: slowly, bureaucratically, with the institutional momentum of a structure that was never designed to respond quickly to anything.]

[He does it anyway. Not out of hope. Out of practice.]

[CAMERA: Slow pull back to the wide doorway shot. Hold three seconds.]

[AUDIO: A school bell rings somewhere in the building — distant, the warning bell, fifteen minutes before classes. Haruki does not look up. He continues sorting.]

[DESIGN NOTE — VOICE: Haruki has been the most verbal NPC in the game outside Mira and Kenji. His epilogue gives him no lines. The character who once explained too much, too fast, in the wrong register has been resolved into the man who simply does the work. Speech is not the resolution. The work is.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Hold on black three seconds.

### FUMIKO

**Location:** Indeterminate — the scene is staged on the published artifact itself. Could be a newspaper on a kitchen table, a phone screen, a tablet in someone's hands. Time of day is ambiguous.
**Runtime:** ~55 seconds
**Format:** Cutscene with text-on-image emphasis. Fumiko does not appear. The story is the resolution.
**Transition in:** Fade-up from black. The first image is the publication artifact — paper or screen — with the headline visible.
**Score:** None — ambient only. If the artifact is paper: the sound of pages, a quiet kitchen or office, no music. If the artifact is digital: the soft device interface sounds of scrolling, a notification dismissed elsewhere. The choice between paper and digital can be a regional/platform variant; both are valid.
**Branching:** Soft. If the player chose to release Fumiko early in Ch 12 (publish-now option), the implied date on the publication artifact is earlier — the story ran during the case rather than after. If the player held Fumiko, the publication is post-arrest, more complete, the headline slightly less urgent. Both versions resolve identically: the story exists, was read, mattered.

[VISUAL: A newspaper. Or a screen. Or both — the story published in the format Fumiko always intended: thorough, sourced, structured. Not a scoop. A record.]

[AUDIO: The handling sound of the medium — paper folded, a screen scrolled. A quiet ambient room. Nothing more.]

[CAMERA: Close-up on the headline. The full byline reads "Arai Fumiko." The dateline is six months ago, or three weeks ago (depending on branch). The format is broadsheet column structure, dense with text.]

[The headline names Endo. The story names the exchange, the garden, the committee, the infrastructure. It names the children — not as victims in a crime report, but as people who saw things and said things and were not heard. It names the adults who didn't listen and the systems that helped them not listen. It is not an accusation. It is documentation.]

[CAMERA: Slow scroll or pan down the article. The text is legible but does not require reading — the player sees the structure of journalism rather than the journalism itself. Section breaks, photographs (small, formal — Endo's headshot, the community center exterior, no images of the children), pull-quotes.]

[DESIGN NOTE — PHOTOGRAPHY IN THE ARTICLE: Mira's photograph does not appear in Fumiko's published story. Reiko withheld permission. Fumiko respected this. The story names Mira, but does not display her face. This is one of Fumiko's quiet acts of ethical care — and it is invisible unless the player notices the absence.]

[Fumiko published the full story. The one she spent years assembling, the one she held for forty-eight hours while Kenji built the case, the one that required a detective to confirm what a journalist had been tracking for a decade.]

[CAMERA: Pull back from the article. The artifact sits in frame — paper on a table, screen on a desk. A hand may briefly enter the edge of frame to turn a page or set the device down. The hand is unidentified — it is not Fumiko's, not Kenji's, not anyone the player knows. It is a reader. The article reached someone.]

[Someone listened. Not just Kenji — the readers. The community. The parents who sent their children to schools overseen by a committee that dismissed every behavioral report. The neighbors who admired a garden they didn't understand. The story doesn't heal Yanagi. It informs it. Which is all journalism was ever supposed to do.]

[AUDIO: A pause. Then the sound of the page turning, or the screen going dark. The reader has finished.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade to black. Hold on black four seconds — slightly longer than the standard three. The Nishida Question is the next scene, and the held black gives it room to feel different.

### THE NISHIDA QUESTION

**Location:** N/A — this is a structural/thematic beat staged across multiple visual modes. See FORMAT NOTE below.
**Runtime:** ~95 seconds (the longest epilogue scene)
**Format:** Mixed mode — text-on-black for the framing prose, brief montage interlude for the unnamed call, return to text-on-black for the closing reflection. No interactive elements. No spoken dialogue.
**Transition in:** Extended hold on black after Fumiko (four seconds). Then text appears, white on black, set in the same body font used for in-game notebook entries. The text does not animate in — it is present when the fade resolves. The reader arrives mid-thought, the way community memory arrives mid-thought.
**Score:** A single sustained low tone — barely perceptible, more pressure than music. This is the only epilogue scene that uses any score element, and the score is itself almost silence. The tone enters with the first text and persists through the unnamed call, dropping out for the closing lines. If the scene cannot support a single tone, default to ambient room tone (the same low hum used during exchange room scenes in Chapters 10–12). The exchange's residue, carried into the aftermath.
**Branching:** YES — gated. This scene only fires if the player reached the deep Ogawa thread (the Nishida sub-layer) in Ch 9. Players who pursued only the standard Ogawa thread skip this scene entirely. The epilogue continues directly from Fumiko to Aizawa.

  - **Skipped variant:** No transition needed. Fumiko's fade-out blends into Aizawa's fade-in. The player who did not surface the Nishida thread does not learn it now. The question remains buried for them — which is itself thematically appropriate.
  - **Played variant:** Full scene as specified below.

[FORMAT NOTE — STAGING: The Nishida Question is the most unusual epilogue beat because it is not a character resolution. It is a community-scale aftermath. The staging breaks the per-character cutscene pattern deliberately — to mark this as a different kind of beat, requiring a different mode. The text-on-black sections function as voiceover would, but without a voice — the player reads them in their own internal voice, the way Mira's notebook entries read. The unnamed call montage is the only scene in the entire epilogue that depicts a phone call, and it is depicted without showing either party clearly. This is intentional: the Nishida Question is about the cost of the truth being public, and the call is the moment of cost.]

[VISUAL: Black. White text appears, set in the in-game body font.]

[The trial opened the files. Not all of them — but enough. The safety council's internal records named every concern Endo had suppressed, every report he had redirected, every pattern he had managed. Among them: a prior concern about a teacher's aide, filed by Ms. Ogawa, the earliest documented instance of the system Endo built.]

[AUDIO: The sustained low tone enters under the first text block. Barely audible. A pressure rather than a sound.]

[Fumiko named the mechanism, not the man. Her story referenced "a staff member whose reassignment was the first recorded suppression." She verified facts, not suspicion. She was careful.]

[The community was not careful.]

[CAMERA: The text dissolves to black. Three-second hold. Then a brief montage begins.]

[VISUAL — MONTAGE (approximately 25 seconds): A series of brief, deliberately ordinary shots. A family at a dinner table, laughing about something inconsequential — they are not the family in the story. A teacher's aide volunteer in a parents' group photograph from the school newsletter, his face out of focus. A community center bulletin board showing the trial coverage. A telephone on a side table in an unfamiliar apartment. The phone begins to ring.]

[AUDIO: The montage carries no music. The dinner table conversation is muted to indistinct cadence. The photograph is silent. The bulletin board is silent. The telephone rings — once, twice. A hand picks up the receiver.]

[Parents remembered the aide. Parents remembered which children had been in the tutoring program. Two weeks after the trial began, a man in another city received a phone call from a former colleague. The question was never finished. He hung up. Called back to say it shouldn't have been asked. Hung up again.]

[AUDIO: The call's audio is heard at low volume, with no clear words — only the cadence of two voices, hesitation, an intake of breath, a question half-spoken, the click of a receiver. The second click, the hang-up of the apology call, is the loudest sound in the entire scene. It is a small click. It is enormous in this context.]

[DESIGN NOTE — VOICE: The unnamed man is not voiced. The colleague is not voiced. The voices in the call are smeared into background, deliberately unintelligible. This is the only call in the game that the player is not allowed to hear clearly. The player has heard every other call in twelve chapters. This one is private — and the privacy is the violation. The player understands what was asked without hearing it.]

[VISUAL: Return to black. Text resumes.]

[The question Endo buried — was Nishida dangerous, or was he a patient man misread by a careful teacher? — was never answered. It was only made public. The ambiguity Endo preserved for years, that cost Ogawa her career, that became the foundation of a philosophy that produced a dead child and a missing boy — that ambiguity now belonged to the community. And the community didn't know what to do with it.]

[No one was charged. No family pulled their children. No man was found dead in an apartment. Fujisawa did not repeat.]

[AUDIO: The sustained tone drops out under the next text block. The remainder plays in true silence.]

[But it rhymed. A name adjacent to an investigation. A question that couldn't be answered. A man whose guilt was never established and whose innocence was never confirmed, living in the space between.]

[The truth saved a boy and arrested a monster. It also unsealed a question that had been sealed for a reason, and the unsealing cut someone whose name should probably never have been in the file.]

[Endo would have predicted this. He would have been right about the mechanism.]

[He would still have been wrong about everything else.]

[CAMERA: The final line holds in white-on-black for five seconds. The longest single text hold in the epilogue. The player must sit with the closing thesis.]

[DESIGN NOTE — THEMATIC FUNCTION: This scene is the game's only direct moral concession to its villain. Endo was right about one thing. The player who reached this scene earned the right to know that. The player who did not is spared the complication. Both readings are valid endings. The game does not adjudicate which path is correct — that is the point.]

**Transition out:** Slow fade from white-on-black to true black. Hold five seconds. The Nishida Question is given the most darkness around it of any beat in the epilogue. The community-scale weight requires it.

### AIZAWA

**Location:** Yanagi Municipal Elementary — administrative office, Aizawa's desk (Ch 5/Ch 8 setting). Mid-day. School in session, audible faintly through the walls.
**Runtime:** ~55 seconds

[CAMERA: Medium-wide shot from across the yard. Rina is in the center of her cluster. The camera does not push in. We are observing from outside the social group — the same observational position the game has held throughout, where the player sees the social fabric without belonging to it.]

[CAMERA: Hold for ten seconds. The image is unchanging. The player sits with the desk.]

[The phone rings once.]

[AUDIO: A single phone ring. The exact same ring sound used in Chapter 1's cold open — the specific tone, the specific duration, the specific way it cuts through the silence. The ring is not modified. It is the same ring. If the audio is not bit-identical, it is wrong.]

[CAMERA: Hold. The phone does not move. The lamp does not flicker. The image is static.]

[Nobody picks up.]

[CAMERA: Hold for fifteen seconds after the ring. The silence after must be longer than the player expects. The longer the hold, the more the player understands what has changed: in the cold open, the player picked up the phone and the game began. In the closing, the phone rings and nobody picks up. The game does not begin again. Mira has called and she is calling someone else, or she is calling no one, or it is not Mira at all — it is just a phone ringing in an empty room, the way phones ring sometimes, the way they did before her and will after her.]

[DESIGN NOTE — THE FINAL INTERPRETATION: The ring's meaning is left to the player. Three readings are valid:
1. Mira persists in some residual form, still calling, still waiting for someone to pick up. The cycle continues.
2. It is not Mira. It is another case, another child, another voice in the static. The work is not finished.
3. It is no one. Phones ring in empty rooms. The world makes sounds that do not require a listener.

[DESIGN NOTE — VOICE: Mira does NOT speak in the title screen return. Even if she is the implied caller, she does not get to speak. The game ends with a ringing phone and a silent room. [CAMERA: After the fifteen-second post-ring hold, the title text "DEAD RINGER" fades up over the held frame. The composition is now the formal title screen. The menu UI follows three seconds later — small, lower-third, unobtrusive: NEW GAME / CONTINUE / CREDITS / QUIT.]

**Transition out:** None. The title screen is the final state. The player chooses what to do next, or chooses to do nothing. The game does not move on without them.

## EPILOGUE PRODUCTION NOTES (Master)

### AUDIO DESIGN PRINCIPLES

**1. Score is contraband.** The only score cue in the entire epilogue is the sustained low tone in the Nishida Question, and that tone is barely music. Every other scene plays in pure ambient. If a producer or composer requests additional cues, refuse. The game has earned the silence.

**2. Mira's voice is gone.** No dialogue, no whispers, no callbacks via flashback audio. The italicized line in Kenji's scene is text only. If a future production decision adds VO, it breaks the contract the game made with the player in Ch 12.

**3. Diegetic sound carries the emotion.** A kettle clicking off (Reiko), a wind chime (Yui, Kaito), a phone hang-up (Nishida), a drawer sliding (Kenji), a single phone ring (title screen). These small mechanical sounds are the score. Treat them as such.

**4. Silence is structural.** The held blacks between scenes, the long quiet hold on Kenji's final desk, the post-ring silence on the title screen — these are not transitions, they are content. Do not shorten them in editing.

### VISUAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES

**1. Locked compositions.** The epilogue is the slowest visual register in the game. Cuts are infrequent. Pushes are slow. There is no handheld and no canted angle. The world has stilled and the camera reflects this.

**2. NPCs are present but not foregrounded.** Reiko, Aizawa, Haruki, Kaito, Rina, and Kenji are observed from middle distance. The camera does not dwell on faces. The closest character close-ups are reserved for the essay (Sora) and the bench scratch (Doi) — both objects, not faces. Even Sora himself is not shown in close-up. The epilogue is about resolution, not portraiture.

**3. The seasonal continuity is warm.** The game opens in winter (3:47 AM in cold weather, Ch 1). Six months later places the epilogue in summer. Cicadas, warm light, open windows, daylight that lasts. The visual warmth is not comfort; it is the indifferent passage of time.
